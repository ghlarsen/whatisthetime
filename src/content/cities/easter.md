Easter Island, known to its Rapa Nui people as Te Pito o Te Henua ("the navel of the world"), sits in the South Pacific at 27 degrees south, 3,700 kilometers west of the Chilean coast. It is one of the most isolated permanently inhabited places on Earth. The IANA identifier `Pacific/Easter` gives the island its own timezone, reflecting its unique geographic position.

## time two hours behind the mainland

Easter Island observes Chilean DST, shifting between UTC-6 (Easter Island Standard Time) and UTC-5 (Easter Island Summer Time) following mainland Chile's schedule, adjusted for the Southern Hemisphere. In practice, the island sits 2 hours behind mainland Chile in winter and 1 hour behind in summer, when Chile also shifts its clocks.

Hanga Roa, the island's only town, has a population of roughly 7,500, many of whom are Rapa Nui descended from the people who carved the famous moai statues. The island's rhythm has been shaped more by its isolation and the rhythm of the Pacific than by any administrative timezone. Flights from Santiago arrive twice a week. Supply ships come monthly. Between those arrivals, the island runs on its own time in a deeper sense than any IANA entry captures.
