Geneva keeps Central European Time: UTC+1 in winter, UTC+2 in summer under daylight saving. The same clock as [Zurich](/zurich), [Paris](/paris), and [Berlin](/berlin). The city changes clocks in late March and late October, in line with EU conventions.

The irony is not subtle. Geneva is the watchmaking capital of the world, home to Rolex, Patek Philippe, and dozens of other precision manufacturers. The city that sells time to the world runs on the same Central European standard as its neighbors.

## Calvin, the consistory, and bell discipline

Geneva's relationship with time was shaped by John Calvin, who ran the city's religious government from 1541 until his death in 1564. The consistory logged attendance at sermons. Taverns had closing hours. Social discipline required knowing, and keeping, the time. The city's church bells rang a structured schedule that organized daily life for two centuries.

That culture of punctuality did not disappear. It migrated into the watchmaking workshops that began flourishing in the seventeenth century, when French Huguenot craftsmen fled religious persecution and arrived in Geneva with their skills.

## The international city's clock

Geneva hosts the United Nations Office, the World Health Organization, the International Red Cross, and hundreds of NGOs. These institutions run on a 24-hour international schedule, with meetings that span timezones. The city's civil time is UTC+1/+2, but its institutional culture runs on global time constantly.
