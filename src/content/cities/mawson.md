Mawson Station is the oldest continuously operated Antarctic research station, established by Australia in 1954. It runs on UTC+5, chosen for alignment with the Australian Antarctic Division's administrative base in Hobart, Tasmania, which is at UTC+10 or UTC+11, though five hours rather than ten reflects a practical compromise for coordination.

In Antarctica, timezone choice is almost philosophical. At the South Pole, every longitude converges. At coastal stations, the sun may not set for months or may not rise for months. The clock's relationship to solar time is severed. Stations choose offsets for administrative convenience, synchronizing with their home countries or support bases.

Mawson sits in MacRobertson Land at 67 degrees south latitude. During Antarctic summer, the sun does not set. During polar winter, it does not rise. The 12 to 20 people who winter over at Mawson maintain a civil schedule against total solar ambiguity.

The station is named for the Australian explorer Sir Douglas Mawson, who led several Antarctic expeditions in the early twentieth century.
