Thessaloníki is Greece's second city, and it carries its history more visibly than most. Byzantine churches, Ottoman mosques, Roman arches, and a Jewish quarter that was one of the most significant in the world until 1943 all exist within a few kilometers of each other. The city spent centuries under Ottoman rule, became part of [Greece](/country/greece) only in 1912, and has always had a cosmopolitan, layered quality.

The clock here is Eastern European Time, UTC+2 in winter and UTC+3 during summer DST, identical to [Athens](/athens) and the rest of Greece. At 40 degrees north, Thessaloníki gets meaningful daylight variation: long summer evenings that feed the outdoor café culture, and winter afternoons that go dark by 5pm.

## late meals and layered history

Greek mealtimes run later than Northern European norms, a pattern that holds in Thessaloníki. Lunch happens closer to 2pm, dinner rarely before 9pm. The clock says one thing; the street says another.

The White Tower on the waterfront was built by the Ottomans in the 15th century and served as a prison. Now it's the city's symbol. Sunset over the Thermaic Gulf from the tower's base is one of those daily events that draws the same crowd, year after year, at whatever hour the sun chooses.

[9am in Thessaloníki is 7am in London](/when/9am-thessaloniki/london). [Istanbul](/istanbul) just across the border keeps the same clock.
