San Juan has been continuously inhabited since 1521, making it the oldest city under US jurisdiction. Old San Juan, the original walled city on the island at the harbor entrance, retains its Spanish colonial grid: colorful buildings, cobblestoned streets, two massive fortresses (El Morro and San Cristóbal) at the headlands. The walls still stand.

[Puerto Rico](/country/puerto-rico) uses Atlantic Standard Time, UTC-4, permanently. No daylight saving, no clock changes. San Juan and [Ponce](/ponce) share the same fixed offset year-round. In winter, this means Puerto Rico is one hour ahead of New York. In summer, when New York advances to UTC-4, the clocks match.

Puerto Rico's relationship with time includes its diaspora: millions of Puerto Ricans live on the US mainland, mostly in New York and Florida. The connection between San Juan and New York is so constant that the one-hour winter difference and summer alignment shape daily communication, TV scheduling, and business hours.

The bioluminescent bay at Mosquito Bay on Vieques island (accessible from San Juan) is best seen in darkness, around midnight. The water glows blue-green when disturbed, an effect from billions of dinoflagellates. The bay runs on its own biological schedule, brightest on moonless nights.
