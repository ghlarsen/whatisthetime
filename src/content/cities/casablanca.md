Casablanca is Morocco's economic capital and largest city, running on Africa/Casablanca. Morocco's timezone is one of the world's more complex arrangements: the country observes a form of daylight saving time, but with an annual exception during Ramadan when it reverts to a lower offset. The result is a schedule that changes three or four times per year.

The general pattern: Morocco uses UTC+1 for most of the year (Western European Summer Time) and switches briefly to UTC+0 during Ramadan before returning to UTC+1. The exact dates change every year because Ramadan follows the Islamic lunar calendar, advancing about 11 days annually. Some years the Ramadan reversal creates two clock changes in spring, two in autumn.

## Why the Ramadan exception?

The stated reason is to ease the burden of fasting. During Ramadan, Muslims fast from dawn until sunset. Morocco's sunset occurs later when the clock is at UTC+1 (daylight saving). By reverting to UTC+0 during Ramadan, the government effectively makes the fasting period feel slightly shorter by bringing sunset earlier in the clock day. The practical effect varies, but the symbolic acknowledgment of Ramadan in the timezone policy is clear.

This creates genuine scheduling challenges for international business. Companies in Casablanca dealing with European partners must track Morocco's specific clock against the EU's DST schedule. They are not always in sync.

## Africa's financial capital

Casablanca is the financial hub for North and West Africa. The Casablanca Finance City (CFC) hosts African subsidiaries of international banks, investment firms, and multinational companies. These firms manage portfolios across multiple African timezones. Casablanca's shifting clock is one more complexity in that equation.

The [time conversion from Casablanca to London](/when/9am-casablanca/london) varies: same time or one hour ahead, depending on the season and whether Ramadan is active.
