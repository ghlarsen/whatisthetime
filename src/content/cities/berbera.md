Berbera is the principal port city of Somaliland, the self-declared republic that has governed itself separately from the rest of Somalia since 1991. The city keeps East Africa Time: UTC+3, shared with [Mogadishu](/mogadishu) and East Africa broadly. No daylight saving time is observed.

At roughly 10 degrees north latitude, near the Gulf of Aden, Berbera has very short twilight periods. The sun rises and sets quickly at this equatorial proximity. Daylight hours vary only slightly through the year, sunrise staying between 5:30am and 6:30am depending on season. The heat is intense, particularly from May to September, when temperatures regularly exceed 40 degrees Celsius.

## Gulf of Aden gateway

Berbera's strategic position on the Gulf of Aden has made it important for centuries. Arab traders used it as a stopping point. The British Empire made it the capital of British Somaliland in the nineteenth century. In the 1970s, the Soviet Union built a large naval facility here; after Somalia switched Cold War allegiances, the United States took it over. The base shaped the city's development for decades.

Today the port is operated under a deal with DP World, the Dubai-based port operator, and handles trade for landlocked Ethiopia as an alternative to Djibouti's port. The strategic logic that made Berbera important 200 years ago still applies.

## Somaliland's separate clock

Somaliland keeps the same timezone as Somalia despite operating as a completely separate administration with its own currency, passports, and government institutions. The UTC+3 offset is practical: Somaliland's trade partners and neighbors all use it. [Djibouti](/djibouti) uses UTC+3. Ethiopia uses UTC+3. The regional clock is consistent even where political recognition is not.

The [time difference between Berbera and London](/when/9am-berbera/london) is three hours in winter, two hours in summer when the UK observes BST.
