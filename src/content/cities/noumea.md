Nouméa is the capital of New Caledonia, a French special collectivity in the southwest Pacific. The city sits on a long peninsula facing a protected lagoon, and the lagoon itself, part of the largest coral reef system outside Australia, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The light on the water in the late afternoon, coming off the nickel-red hills behind the city, has a quality that would be remarkable anywhere.

New Caledonia uses New Caledonia Time, NCT, UTC+11, year-round with no daylight saving. The IANA identifier is Pacific/Noumea. At 22 degrees south, the island experiences moderate seasonal variation: summer days push toward 13.5 hours of light, winter days shorten to under 11. The climate is subtropical and the temperature variation between seasons is mild.

Nouméa is unusual among Pacific capitals for its very French character: baguettes, wine, roundabouts, and a sense of design that distinguishes it from Suva or Port Moresby. But the indigenous Kanak population and their land rights have been central to the territory's political life for decades. The Noumea Accord of 1998, the independence referenda of 2018, 2020, and 2021 (all won by those wishing to remain French), are the political rhythm underlying the city's daily life.

## nickel, independence referenda, and French Pacific life

Nickel mining is the economic foundation. New Caledonia holds roughly a quarter of the world's known nickel reserves, and the smelting operations at Doniambo near Nouméa run around the clock.
