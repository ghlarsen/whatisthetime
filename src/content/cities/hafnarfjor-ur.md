Hafnarfjörður is a town immediately south of Reykjavik, part of the Capital Region of Iceland. It runs on Iceland Time, UTC+0, year-round. Iceland abolished daylight saving in 1968 and has not returned to it.

At UTC+0 and 64 degrees North latitude, Hafnarfjörður experiences some of the most extreme daylight variation of any significant town on Earth. In midsummer, the sun barely dips below the horizon. In midwinter, daylight lasts only four to five hours.

## Why Iceland stays at UTC+0 in summer

Iceland's geographic position is far enough west that UTC+0 is actually ahead of solar time in summer. The sun would suggest UTC-1 is more natural. By staying at UTC+0, Iceland keeps its civil time somewhat aligned with [London](/london) and [Reykjavik](/reykjavik) without the disruption of clock changes.

The decision also reflects a practical reality: in summer, there is so much daylight that a one-hour adjustment barely matters. In winter, it makes no real difference to the very limited daylight hours either way.

## A Viking harbor town

Hafnarfjörður has a natural harbor sheltered by lava fields, which made it one of Iceland's most important trading ports in the medieval period. English and German merchants traded here extensively in the fifteenth century, before the Danish monopoly closed the port to foreign trade.

The town hosts an annual Viking Festival that draws international visitors and celebrates the region's Norse heritage.
