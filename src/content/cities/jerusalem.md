Jerusalem runs on Israel Standard Time, UTC+2 in winter and Israel Daylight Time, UTC+3, in summer. The transition dates are set annually by the Israeli government and can vary to avoid conflicts with Jewish holidays.

At UTC+2, Jerusalem shares its winter clock with [Cairo](/cairo), [Athens](/athens), and [Kyiv](/kyiv). In summer at UTC+3, it matches [Moscow](/moscow) and [Nairobi](/nairobi).

## A city with three calendars

Jerusalem is sacred to Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. Each tradition maintains its own calendar system.

The Jewish calendar is lunisolar, with 12 or 13 months per year. Shabbat begins at sunset on Friday and ends when three stars appear on Saturday night. Jewish holidays, including Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, Passover, and Sukkot, are set by the Hebrew calendar.

The Islamic calendar is purely lunar. Friday midday prayer, Jumuah, is the week's central congregational time. Ramadan, the month of fasting, moves through the Gregorian year over a 33-year cycle.

Christian liturgical calendars, used by the city's Catholic, Greek Orthodox, Armenian, Ethiopian, and other communities, calculate Easter by different methods, meaning Eastern and Western Easter fall on different dates most years.

## UTC and the wall

The different religious communities' control over different quarters and holy sites means that daily schedules in Jerusalem are genuinely fragmented. Market days, prayer hours, and business closures follow different rhythms depending on which community and which part of the city.
