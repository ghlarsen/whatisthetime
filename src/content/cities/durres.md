Durrës, Albania's main port and second-largest city, sits on the Adriatic coast at 41 degrees north. It uses `Europe/Tirane`, CET (UTC+1) in winter and CEST (UTC+2) in summer, following EU-aligned daylight saving rules. Albania is an EU candidate country and has aligned its clock changes with the EU schedule.

Durrës has been inhabited since at least the 7th century BC and was an important Roman colonial city, the starting point of the Via Egnatia, the road that ran east across the Balkans toward Constantinople. The city's port has been active for over two millennia: first Greco-Roman amphorae, then Venetian galleys, Ottoman galleons, and today container ships and Adriatic ferry services to Bari and Ancona.

The ferry crossing to Bari is the most common way to travel between Albania and Italy. The journey takes about 9 hours, and since both cities use Central European Time, passengers don't need to adjust their clocks. They leave Durrës and arrive in Bari at the same local time, which is unusual for a sea crossing between countries. The clock is one of the few continuities on a route that crosses a genuine cultural divide.
