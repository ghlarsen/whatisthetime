Khabarovsk sits on the Amur River, at the border between Russia and China, where the river bends sharply south before continuing to the sea. The city is 8,500 kilometers from Moscow and uses Vladivostok Time, UTC+10. Since Russia abolished daylight saving in 2014, the clocks here have not moved.

## Trans-Siberian junction, permanent winter time

The permanent UTC+10 setting means that in summer, when Khabarovsk's latitude (about 48 degrees north) produces long evenings, the light advantage is not captured by advancing the clock. Evenings are bright, but not as extended as they would be under DST. The sun sets around 8:30pm in late June rather than the 9:30pm a clock change would produce.

Khabarovsk and the nearby [Khabarovsk Vtoroy](/khabarovsk-vtoroy) rail district keep the same time, as does [Vladivostok](/vladivostok) further south on the coast. The shared timezone simplifies logistics along the Trans-Siberian Railway, which passes through this region and has run on consistent Moscow Time for ticketing purposes since the Soviet era, even as local time differs by seven hours.

The city's latitude and continental position produce extremes: winter temperatures can drop below -30C, summer can push +35C. The same clock runs through all of it.

[9am in Khabarovsk is midnight in London](/when/9am-khabarovsk/london), ten hours ahead of GMT.
