Harare is the capital and largest city of Zimbabwe, sitting on a high plateau at about 1,500 meters elevation. It runs on Central Africa Time, UTC+2, year-round, the same as [Johannesburg](/johannesburg) and [Lusaka](/lusaka). No daylight saving.

At UTC+2 and 17.8 degrees South latitude, Harare's solar noon falls at about 12:10pm, a good natural fit. The high altitude moderates temperatures, making Harare one of the more temperate capitals in Africa.

## Zimbabwe's agricultural calendar

Zimbabwe's economy has historically been tied to agriculture, and the farming calendar structures the year more than any civil schedule. The rainy season runs from November to March. Planting, growing, and harvesting create seasonal rhythms that Central Africa Time serves well: predictable daylight, no clock changes, stable schedules.

The tobacco auction season, historically in March and April, once brought farmers and buyers from across the region to Harare in a concentrated annual event. The auction floor established prices and set the commercial rhythm for the year.

## The Zimbabwe dollar and time uncertainty

Zimbabwe's hyperinflation crisis of the late 2000s created a different kind of time uncertainty. When prices changed hourly and the currency lost value by the hour, economic planning on any timescale became nearly impossible. The country briefly used foreign currencies and eventually adopted a new currency. The civil clock remained stable even when economic time felt completely disoriented.
