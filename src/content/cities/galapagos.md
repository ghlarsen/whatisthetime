The Galapagos Islands, Ecuador's Pacific archipelago 1,000 kilometers offshore, run on UTC-6 year-round under `Pacific/Galapagos`. This puts them one hour behind mainland Ecuador, which uses UTC-5 (under `America/Guayaquil`). No daylight saving is observed. The Galapagos's separate timezone reflects their longitude: they sit about 15 degrees west of the mainland, and UTC-6 is a better solar fit than UTC-5.

The islands are famous for Charles Darwin's 1835 visit and the evolutionary observations he made there, published in *On the Origin of Species* in 1859. Darwin's finches, marine iguanas, and giant tortoises exist because the islands' isolation allowed species to evolve without competition from mainland fauna. The same isolation that shaped evolutionary history created the conditions for a separate timezone.

Puerto Ayora on Santa Cruz Island, the main inhabited center, runs on GALT. Scientific researchers at the Charles Darwin Research Station coordinate with institutions in [Quito](/quito), the US, and Europe across multiple timezone offsets. The daily rhythm of the Galapagos is shaped by the sun (which rises around 6am year-round), the tides (which determine when boats can access certain landing sites), and the tourist schedule (which departs from Ecuador's mainland on GALT-plus-one). The clock says one thing; the wildlife operates on evolutionary time.
