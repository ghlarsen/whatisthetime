Arequipa is called "the White City" for its buildings made from white sillar, a volcanic stone quarried from the surrounding highlands. The city sits at 2,335 meters elevation, framed by three volcanoes: El Misti (5,822m), Chachani (6,057m), and Pichu Pichu (5,664m). On clear mornings, the volcanoes catch the first light while the city below is still in shadow.

Peru runs on UTC-5 year-round. No daylight saving. The IANA identifier is America/Lima, used for all of Peru. [Lima](/lima) and Arequipa share the same clock.

At 16 degrees south latitude, Peru's daylight variation is modest. December days run about 13 hours; June days run about 11 hours. The seasonal argument for adjusting clocks is weak. Peru tried daylight saving briefly in the early 1990s and abandoned it.

Arequipa's altitude creates a distinct daily rhythm. Mornings are bright and cool. By midday, the sun is intense at this elevation with less atmosphere to filter it. Afternoons can bring clouds building around the volcanoes. The city's plaza, the Plaza de Armas, is one of South America's finest colonial squares, and it fills with life in the cooler evening hours.

The city has a reputation for being more conservative and independent-minded than Lima, sometimes called the "Republic of Arequipa" by locals. This character extends to its rhythms: a city that keeps its own pace.
