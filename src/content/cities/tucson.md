Tucson is a desert city in southern Arizona at about 800 meters elevation, ringed by five mountain ranges. It has held the same time since Arizona made a definitive decision in 1968 to opt out of daylight saving time, citing the desert heat: why would you want an extra hour of afternoon sun in a place where summer temperatures exceed 40°C?

The practical result is that Tucson runs on Mountain Standard Time, UTC-7, year-round. In the summer months, when most of the [United States](/country/united-states) shifts to daylight saving, Tucson is on the same clock as California (Pacific Daylight Time). In winter, it's on Mountain time. The city does not move its clocks; the relative position of its neighbors does.

Within Arizona, the Navajo Nation is an exception. The Navajo reservation, which extends into Utah and New Mexico, does observe DST, meaning parts of the state within the Navajo lands switch while the rest of Arizona does not.

Tucson's saguaro cactus forests and the University of Arizona's astronomy facilities give it a particular interest in solar cycles. The saguaro blooms between late April and June, timed to desert heat rather than civil dates. The observatories at Kitt Peak, southwest of the city, operate on precise astronomical time.

[9am in Tucson is 4pm in London](/when/9am-tucson/london). [Phoenix](/phoenix) shares the permanent UTC-7 clock.
