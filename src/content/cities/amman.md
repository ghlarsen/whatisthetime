Amman is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world. The biblical city of Rabbah, the Roman Philadelphia, the medieval Islamic city of Amman, all layered over one another on seven hills above the Jordan Valley. The modern Jordanian capital is a sprawling, white-stone city that has grown dramatically since the country's independence in 1946.

Jordan uses Eastern European Time, UTC+2, in winter and observes daylight saving by advancing to UTC+3 in summer. The IANA identifier is Asia/Amman. This places Jordan in sync with [Athens](/athens) and [Cairo](/cairo) in winter (Egypt on permanent UTC+2 without DST) and with [Beirut](/beirut) and [Damascus](/damascus) through the summer.

The daylight saving transition in Jordan has historically occurred at the last Friday of March and the last Friday of September, following Islamic prayer scheduling logic. The Friday evening timing minimizes disruption to the working week.

## Evening city, late nights

Amman's daily rhythm reflects both its climate and its culture. The city wakes early for morning prayers, cools down during the early afternoon especially in summer, and comes fully alive in the evenings. Restaurants and cafes fill after 9pm. The city's social life runs late by Western standards.

The Friday-Saturday weekend (Jordan switched from Thursday-Friday in 2000) means the workweek starts Sunday, creating a one-day offset from European business schedules.

[9am in Amman is 7am in London](/when/9am-amman/london) during European winter.
