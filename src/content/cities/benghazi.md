Benghazi, Libya's second-largest city and the historical capital of Cyrenaica, keeps time on Africa/Tripoli: UTC+2, year-round. The country does not observe daylight saving time. Libya tried DST briefly in 2012 and 2013 during the post-Gaddafi transitional period but abandoned it after public resistance and practical confusion in a country already dealing with far more severe instability.

The UTC+2 offset puts Benghazi on the same clock as much of Eastern Europe and Central Africa. It also means solar noon falls close to 1pm in winter and close to 2pm in summer, since Libya does not adjust. The sun's arc aligns reasonably well with the clock through winter months; summer afternoons feel long and bright.

## The divided country

Since 2014, Libya has been functionally split between administrations in Tripoli, in the west, and governments based in or associated with Benghazi and the east. Despite this political fracture, both sides use UTC+2. In a country divided by armed conflict, a shared clock is one of the few genuinely unified things remaining.

Benghazi was the cradle of the 2011 uprising that ended Gaddafi's 42-year rule. The city has been at the center of Libyan political life, for better and worse, ever since. Its port, its university, and its commercial district have all been shaped by years of conflict, reconstruction, and again conflict.

## Mediterranean latitude, desert heat

At roughly 32 degrees north, Benghazi has a Mediterranean climate along its coast that shifts quickly to desert conditions inland. Sunrise in June arrives around 5:30am local time. The afternoon heat in summer drives people indoors from roughly noon until 4pm, a rhythm that has shaped daily schedules for generations regardless of what the clock shows.

The [time difference between Benghazi and London](/when/9am-benghazi/london) is two hours in winter, one hour in summer when the UK observes BST.
