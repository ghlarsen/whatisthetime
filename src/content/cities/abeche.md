Abéché sits near the edge of Chad's eastern border, close enough to Sudan to feel the pull of a different region entirely. It is the country's fourth-largest city and has been a trading post, a sultanate capital, and a refugee hub. Time here is shaped less by timezones and more by the sun and the heat.

The city runs on West Africa Time, UTC+1, the same offset as [N'Djamena](/ndjamena), Chad's capital 900 kilometers to the west. No daylight saving, no seasonal adjustment. The clock stays still while the days lengthen and shorten around it.

## Life by the sun's rhythm

Dawn is the city's most active hour. Markets open early, before the Sahel heat builds. By midday, the pace slows. Movement picks up again in the late afternoon as temperatures ease. This rhythm, call it solar time in practice regardless of what the clock says, has governed daily life here for generations longer than any standardized timezone.

[Chad](/country/chad) adopted UTC+1 as its single national timezone, which makes coordination with neighboring Sudan (UTC+2) require a one-hour mental adjustment for anyone crossing the border near Abéché.
