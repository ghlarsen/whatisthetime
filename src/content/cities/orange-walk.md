Orange Walk sits in the north of Belize, a sugarcane and citrus farming district on the New River, close to the Mexican border. Belize Standard Time is UTC-6 year-round, with no daylight saving. The country chose to stay on permanent Central Standard Time rather than adjust seasonally, aligning itself practically with the Mexican states just across the border.

At 18 degrees north, Orange Walk's daylight varies modestly: around 11 hours in December and 13 hours in June. The tropical climate means the light is intense regardless of season, and the real rhythm of the agricultural year is driven by the sugarcane harvest, which runs from January through June, and the rainy season that follows.

The Crooked Tree Wildlife Sanctuary to the south and the Maya ruins at Lamanai up the New River are the region's main draws for outside visitors. Lamanai, an active Maya city from around 1500 BC until the Spanish period, gives the Orange Walk area a depth of human time that puts the colonial-era sugar economy in perspective.

Orange Walk's population includes Maya communities, Mennonite farming settlements, Garifuna, and the mixed Creole and Mestizo communities that characterize northern Belize. Multiple cultures, one clock, one language of commerce.
