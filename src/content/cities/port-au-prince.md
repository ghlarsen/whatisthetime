Port-au-Prince follows Eastern Time, the same offset as New York, Miami, and the major US cities that host much of the Haitian diaspora. UTC-5 in winter (EST), UTC-4 in summer (EDT). The shared clock is practically useful: Haiti's connections to its diaspora, concentrated in South Florida and New York, run on coordinated schedules.

[Haiti](/country/haiti) and [Port-de-Paix](/port-de-paix) to the north share this schedule across the country. The island of Hispaniola holds an unusual situation: Haiti on the west uses Eastern Time, while the Dominican Republic on the east uses Atlantic Standard Time (UTC-4 year-round). Two countries on one island, offset from each other by an hour in winter.

Port-au-Prince sits at 18 degrees north, close enough to the equator that daylight variation is modest. Sunrise comes between 5:45am and 6:30am, sunset between 5:50pm and 7:00pm. The city's famous traffic, the tap-taps crowding the streets, the markets that overflow sidewalks into roads, runs on a rhythm that survives every disruption the city has faced.

The earthquake of January 12, 2010, struck at 4:53pm local time. The moment is specific in every account of it. Time, in a city that has survived repeated catastrophe, is marked by what happened before and after.
