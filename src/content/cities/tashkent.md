Tashkent has been a crossroads for two thousand years. Silk Road caravans stopped here, armies passed through, empires dissolved and reformed around it. The city today is Central Asia's largest, a metropolis of several million that carries Soviet-era architecture alongside ancient mosques and a remarkably modern metro system.

[Uzbekistan](/country/uzbekistan) runs on UTC+5, year-round, with no daylight saving time. This puts Tashkent five hours ahead of London (or four in British summer time) and roughly aligned with Pakistan and parts of South Asia. The decision to skip DST is standard for Central Asian former Soviet states, most of which abandoned the practice in the 1990s and haven't looked back.

At 41 degrees north, Tashkent gets noticeable seasonal daylight variation without the extremes of northern latitudes. Summers bring long, warm evenings. The city comes alive after dark in the heat months, with evening tea houses and market activity running well past what the clock would suggest as a reasonable hour.

The 1966 earthquake that leveled much of old Tashkent reset the city's physical form. The Soviet reconstruction gave it wide boulevards and the metro. Time, for the city, moved in abrupt chapters.

[9am in Tashkent is 4am in London](/when/9am-tashkent/london). Neighboring cities on UTC+5 include [Samarkand](/samarkand) to the southwest.
