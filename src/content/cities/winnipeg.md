Winnipeg is approximately equidistant from the Atlantic and Pacific coasts of [Canada](/country/canada), placing it at the geographic heart of the country. The city sits at 49 degrees north on the Canadian Prairie, where the flat landscape extends to every horizon and the sky is enormous.

Central Time, UTC-6 in winter and UTC-5 during summer DST, governs the clock. The seasonal daylight variation at 49 degrees is significant: summer evenings stretch past 9:30pm while winter brings darkness before 5pm. The prairie latitude amplifies the contrast because there are no mountains to block morning or evening light.

Winnipeg has some of the most extreme temperature swings of any major city in the world. Winters regularly reach -30°C or colder. Summers can hit 35°C. The city has developed an extensive system of climate-controlled pedestrian connections (the Winnipeg Walkway) linking downtown buildings, a practical response to winters severe enough that outdoor time is genuinely limited.

The Red River Flood of 1997, and the city's subsequent construction of a massive floodway, demonstrated Winnipeg's willingness to engineer around natural cycles rather than simply accept them.

The city's large Indigenous and Métis population maintains cultural calendars connected to the Red River and the land that run in parallel to the civil clock.

[9am in Winnipeg is 3pm in London](/when/9am-winnipeg/london). [Regina](/regina) in Saskatchewan nearby uses Central Time as well.
