Bergen is Norway's second city, a fjord port on the country's southwestern coast. It keeps Central European Time alongside [Oslo](/oslo) and the rest of Norway: UTC+1 in winter, UTC+2 in summer with DST observed each spring. But what time means in Bergen changes drastically between seasons.

At 60 degrees north latitude, Bergen experiences some of the most dramatic daylight variation on any inhabited coastline. In June, the sun rises before 5am and sets after 10:30pm. In December, sunrise comes around 9am and sunset arrives before 3:30pm. The same clock shows the same numbers, but the sky looks completely different depending on the month.

## The world's rainiest city

Bergen holds a reputation as one of the rainiest cities in Europe. The mountains surrounding the city, the Bergensdalen valley, trap Atlantic moisture coming off the North Sea. The city averages around 230 rain days per year. Locals develop a particular relationship with the sky: they watch it constantly, dress in layers year-round, and don't let rain interrupt plans the way it might in drier climates.

Fog and low clouds routinely obscure Bergen's famous sunrise in winter. The long June days are cherished partly because clear skies feel earned after months of grey.

## Medieval trade and the Hanseatic League

Bergen was Norway's most important city for centuries. The Hanseatic League, the medieval trading network that connected Baltic and North Sea ports, maintained a trading post (the Kontor) at Bergen's Bryggen wharf from the fourteenth century. Merchants from Bremen, Lübeck, and Hamburg operated here under strict communal rules, including rigidly enforced schedules. Work hours, meal times, and seasonal trade patterns were all standardized. The Bryggen wooden warehouses, now a UNESCO World Heritage site, are a physical record of that regime.

The [time conversion from Bergen to London](/when/9am-bergen/london) is one hour ahead in winter, the same time in summer when both observe DST. [Stockholm](/stockholm) shares Bergen's UTC+1/UTC+2 cycle.
