Belogorsk is a mid-sized Russian city in Amur Oblast, sitting on the flat agricultural plain north of the Amur River, which forms the border with China. It runs on Asia/Chita time: UTC+9. That puts it on the same clock as [Blagoveshchensk](/blagoveshchensk), the nearby regional capital, and [Chita](/chita) to the west.

Russia abolished seasonal clock changes in 2014 under President Putin, who declared the country would stay on "winter time" permanently. The result in Belogorsk is that UTC+9 applies year-round, no exceptions.

## Trans-Siberian junction

Belogorsk's identity is inseparable from the railway. It sits at the junction where the Trans-Siberian Railway meets the Baikal-Amur Mainline (BAM). Trains have been the economic lifeblood of this city since the late nineteenth century. Arrival and departure times here connect directly to schedules that span nine timezones across Russia.

The Trans-Siberian runs on Moscow time throughout, a quirk of Russian railway operations. Station clocks in Belogorsk display both local time (UTC+9) and Moscow time (UTC+3). When a train departs at 14:00 on the board, that means 14:00 Moscow time, which is 20:00 in Belogorsk. Passengers from the west who haven't adjusted mentally arrive confused.

## Latitude and light

Belogorsk sits around 51 degrees north latitude. Summers are bright and warm, with daylight stretching past 9pm local time. Winters are harsh and dark, with sunrise delayed until nearly 9am on the shortest days. The city's agricultural hinterland, fields of soy and wheat, follows natural light cycles that the fixed clock cannot fully smooth out.

The [time difference between Belogorsk and London](/when/9am-belogorsk/london) is eight hours in winter, nine when the UK observes BST.
