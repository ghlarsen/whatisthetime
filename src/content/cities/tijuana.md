Tijuana and San Diego are separated by a border fence and approximately 30 seconds of walking. The San Ysidro port of entry, on the edge of Tijuana, is among the busiest land border crossings in the world by raw crossing volume. Hundreds of thousands of people cross each week, most of them for entirely ordinary reasons: work, shopping, family, medical care.

The two cities share a timezone. Tijuana keeps Pacific Time, UTC-8 in winter and UTC-7 during summer DST, matching San Diego exactly. [Mexico](/country/mexico) generally observes DST on a schedule close to the American calendar in the northwest border region, specifically to maintain alignment with California. For a twin city of this economic density, a timezone divergence would be operationally severe.

The alignment is deliberate and practical. Maquiladora factories on the Mexican side depend on synchronized scheduling with supply chains and management offices in California. Commuters who work in San Diego and live in Tijuana need to show up at the same time as their colleagues.

Tijuana's cultural identity is genuinely its own, not just a San Diego suburb across a line. The food, art, music scene, and the Zona Norte's nightlife have their own character. But the clock is shared, because the economics demand it.

[9am in Tijuana is 5pm in London](/when/9am-tijuana/london). [San Jose](/san-jose-costa-rica) and [Vancouver](/vancouver) share parts of the Pacific timezone range.
