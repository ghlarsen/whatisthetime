Corner Brook, on the west coast of Newfoundland, runs on one of the world's more unusual offsets: UTC-3:30 in winter (Newfoundland Standard Time) and UTC-2:30 in summer (Newfoundland Daylight Time). The half-hour offset puts the island out of sync with nearly every neighbor. The rest of Atlantic Canada ([Dartmouth](/dartmouth) in Nova Scotia, for instance) runs UTC-4 in winter. Newfoundland sits 30 minutes ahead of them.

The offset traces back to the 19th century, when Newfoundland set its clocks by the sun at its capital, St. John's, rather than aligning with the mainland. When standard timezones were introduced, Newfoundland negotiated its own identity, a pattern that would repeat when it joined Canada in 1949. The provincial government has discussed adopting Atlantic Time periodically, but surveys consistently find Newfoundlanders prefer their unusual clock.

Corner Brook sits in a fjord-carved valley on Newfoundland's western coast, with sunsets that arrive noticeably later than the Maritimes and the mainland. On a July evening, the sun sets around 9:15pm NDT. That extra half-hour of evening light is, for many residents, worth the scheduling headaches it creates with the rest of the continent.
