Bucharest runs on Europe/Bucharest: Eastern European Time, UTC+2 in winter, UTC+3 in summer. DST follows EU schedules, springing forward in late March and falling back in late October. Romania is in the eastern block of European time, along with [Sofia](/sofia), [Chisinau](/chisinau), and [Helsinki](/helsinki).

Romania's communist-era leader Nicolae Ceausescu manipulated the clock for political purposes. In 1982, his government moved Romania to a non-standard offset as part of energy conservation measures and an assertion of independence from Soviet time norms. The country briefly observed DST on different dates than its neighbors. After Ceausescu's overthrow and execution in 1989, Romania realigned with European standards.

## Latitude and the eastern clock

Bucharest sits at 44 degrees north latitude. The city has notable seasonal daylight variation. In June, sunset arrives around 9pm. In December, darkness falls before 5pm. The UTC+2 base offset is generous for this longitude: Bucharest is at about 26 degrees east, meaning solar noon falls close to 12pm in winter, but with DST in summer, solar noon arrives around 2pm.

This means Bucharest's summer evenings feel very long. The sun is still up past 9pm local time in June. Outdoor café culture, evening promenades on the Calea Victoriei, and the city's nightlife industry all depend on these extended summer evenings.

## The Paris of the East

Bucharest was historically known as the "Paris of the East" for its belle époque architecture and cosmopolitan culture in the early twentieth century. The communist era transformed large sections of the city with massive socialist-realist building projects, including the Palace of the Parliament, one of the world's largest buildings.

The [time conversion from Bucharest to London](/when/9am-bucharest/london) puts Bucharest two hours ahead in winter, one hour in summer when both observe DST.
