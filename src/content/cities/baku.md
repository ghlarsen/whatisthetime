Baku sits on a peninsula jutting into the Caspian Sea, surrounded by the Absheron plateau. Oil seeps in this region were noted by travelers for centuries, and the modern petroleum industry effectively began here in the 1840s. Baku was supplying half the world's oil at the turn of the 20th century. The old city, the Icherisheher, is a UNESCO World Heritage walled medina at the center of a rapidly modernizing metropolis.

The timezone is Azerbaijan Time, UTC+4 in standard time and UTC+5 during daylight saving. The IANA identifier is Asia/Baku. Azerbaijan observes DST, advancing clocks in late March and returning them in late October.

At UTC+4, Baku is one hour ahead of Moscow in winter and two hours ahead when Azerbaijan shifts to UTC+5 while Russia stays at UTC+3. The Caspian Sea's oil and gas industry creates significant coordination with European and Russian corporate schedules.

[Batumi](/batumi) in Georgia, on the Black Sea coast, uses UTC+4 permanently (Georgia does not observe DST), creating a partial year alignment.

Baku's Flame Towers, three skyscrapers shaped like flames, are lit at night with LED displays visible across the city. Fire has been sacred in this region since Zoroastrian times, when natural gas seeps fed flames that appeared to burn permanently. The city has a layered relationship with fire and, by extension, with time: the ancient and the modern in continuous conversation.

[9am in Baku is 5am in London](/when/9am-baku/london) during European winter.
