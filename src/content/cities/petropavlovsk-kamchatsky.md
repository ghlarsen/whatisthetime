Petropavlovsk-Kamchatsky might be the most dramatic setting of any city on earth. It sits on the shore of Avacha Bay, one of the world's finest natural harbors, surrounded on three sides by active volcanoes. Koryaksky and Avachinsky, both over 3,000 meters, dominate the skyline to the north. On clear days, which are less common than misty ones, the volcanoes reflect in the bay. On days when they're venting, the smoke is visible from the city.

[Russia](/country/russia) abolished daylight saving in 2014. Petropavlovsk-Kamchatsky uses Asia/Kamchatka, UTC+12, year-round. This places it 9 hours ahead of Moscow and, for part of the year, at the same offset as New Zealand, far across the Pacific. In terms of the clock, Russia's eastern outpost shares time with the antipodean world.

At 53 degrees north, the city's seasonal light variation is significant: June brings over 17 hours of daylight, December brings under 7. The Pacific climate, moderated by the Kamchatka Current, makes this less severe than interior Siberia at the same latitude, but winters are still cold and dark.

The city is closed to most foreigners without special permits (changed partially in 2019) due to its historical military significance as a nuclear submarine base during the Cold War. The Pacific Fleet still operates here.

Salmon runs from the rivers emptying into the Pacific nearby are one of the most significant wildlife events in the world, and the bears that congregate to catch them are visible from certain points near the city.
