Darwin, capital of Australia's Northern Territory, runs on UTC+9:30 year-round. The half-hour offset puts it perpetually between neighboring timezone zones. The IANA identifier is `Australia/Darwin`. While South Australia also uses UTC+9:30 as its base, South Australia observes daylight saving, shifting to UTC+10:30 in summer. Darwin does not shift. The Northern Territory has voted against DST repeatedly.

The most recent NT referendum on DST, held in 1992, produced a 55% vote against the change. Opposition arguments cited disruption to the cattle industry (which operates at dawn), children going to school in darkness, and heat: adding extra afternoon sun to an already extreme Top End dry-season temperature was not appealing. The vote settled the matter for decades.

Darwin sits at 12.5 degrees south, close enough to the equator that daylight variation is minimal: about 11.5 hours in June and 12.5 hours in December. The DST debate in Darwin is less about light and more about economics and coordination. The city's nearest major partners are in [Perth](/perth), Southeast Asia, and the mining operations across the NT. All of those are easier to schedule without a moving clock.
