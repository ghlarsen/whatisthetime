Erdenet, in north-central Mongolia, was built in the 1970s around the Erdenet copper mine, a joint Soviet-Mongolian enterprise that is still one of the world's largest copper producers. The city runs on `Asia/Ulaanbaatar`, UTC+8, year-round, following Mongolia's 2017 decision to standardize the country on a single offset without DST.

The copper mine operates continuously in shifts, which means Erdenet's daily rhythm is set by the mine as much as by the sun. The processing plant runs 24 hours a day, and a significant portion of the city's workers rotate through night shifts. UTC+8 is the official clock, but the working life of much of the population operates outside normal daylight hours by necessity.

Erdenet is significantly west of [Ulaanbaatar](/ulaanbaatar), meaning UTC+8 runs somewhat fast relative to the local sun. Solar noon in Erdenet arrives around 12:50pm rather than 12:00pm. This hour-level mismatch is accepted as the cost of a unified national timezone. [Darhan](/darhan), Mongolia's other major northern city, faces the same situation. Both cities are administratively synchronized to the capital while the sun arrives at its own schedule.
