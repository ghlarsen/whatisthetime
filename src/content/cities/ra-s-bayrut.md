Ra's Bayrūt is the headland that gives Beirut its face to the Mediterranean. The Corniche runs along the western shore, the sea wall where Beirutis have walked, jogged, and smoked at sunrise for generations. The American University of Beirut occupies the clifftop overlooking the sea. Hamra, the neighborhood behind it, was Beirut's cultural and intellectual center in the 1960s and 1970s.

[Lebanon](/country/lebanon) uses Eastern European Time, UTC+2 in winter and UTC+3 during summer daylight saving. Ra's Bayrūt follows the same schedule as the rest of Beirut and the country. The Lebanese timezone change has sometimes been fraught: in 2023, a political dispute led the government to delay the spring forward by a month, briefly creating a situation where different religious communities were on different times.

Beirut at 33 degrees north has generous Mediterranean winters: mild, sometimes rainy, rarely cold enough to close the outdoor cafes. Summer is long and warm, the sea swimmable from April through October. The light on the western headland at sunset, hitting the limestone cliffs and the seafront buildings, is one of the city's defining daily moments.

The city has rebuilt from civil war, from explosions, from economic collapse, from more, and continues. Time in Beirut is layered with all of it.
