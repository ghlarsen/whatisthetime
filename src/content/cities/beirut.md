Beirut has been called the Paris of the Middle East, a city of extraordinary resilience that has been rebuilt from rubble more than once. The 15-year civil war ended in 1990. The city rebuilt. The 2006 war caused massive damage. The city rebuilt. The August 2020 port explosion, one of the largest non-nuclear explosions in history, destroyed entire neighborhoods. The city is still rebuilding.

The clock is Eastern European Time, UTC+2 in winter and UTC+3 during daylight saving. The IANA identifier is Asia/Beirut.

Lebanon's DST timing has occasionally differed from the standard EU schedule, sometimes starting or ending early for political or religious reasons (aligning with Orthodox Easter, or with Ramadan schedules). These deviations have been infrequent but notable enough to require IANA database updates.

## Nights out in a city that keeps going

Beirut's daily rhythm is Mediterranean-shifted-later: lunch at 2pm, dinner at 9pm or 10pm, evenings that extend past midnight. The city's cafe culture, nightlife, and restaurant scene are remarkable for a city under the ongoing pressures of economic collapse, political dysfunction, and the aftermath of the port explosion.

[Amman](/amman) is in a neighboring timezone, UTC+2 in winter with DST. The two cities are often discussed in the same breath as the eastern Mediterranean's most cosmopolitan.

[9am in Beirut is 7am in London](/when/9am-beirut/london) during European winter.
