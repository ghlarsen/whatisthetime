Likisá (also Liquiçá) is a coastal town west of [Dili](/dili), Timor-Leste's capital, on the northern coast facing the Ombai Strait. The city runs on Asia/Dili time: UTC+9, year-round. No daylight saving.

Timor-Leste chose UTC+9 on independence in 2002, aligning with the eastern boundary of its small territory and placing the country on the same clock as Japan and South Korea. This offset is somewhat ahead of what the sun would suggest for Dili and Likisá (both at around 125 degrees east, which corresponds more naturally to UTC+8:20). The decision was partly economic: UTC+9 aligns Timor-Leste's business hours with Australian Eastern Time (UTC+10) during AEST, just one hour behind Australia's main eastern cities.

Likisá was the site of a massacre in 1999 during the violence surrounding East Timor's independence referendum. The Catholic church in the town, where refugees had sought shelter, was attacked. The date, April 6, 1999, is a fixed point in the country's reckoning of its own history.

The coast around Likisá is relatively undeveloped, with fishing communities using the narrow coastal shelf. At 8.6 degrees south, the city has near-constant daylength and a tropical climate.

[9am in Likisá is midnight in London](/when/9am-likisa/london), nine hours ahead of GMT.
