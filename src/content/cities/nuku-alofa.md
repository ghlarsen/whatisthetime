Every New Year's Eve, television cameras used to point at Nuku'alofa. Tonga, sitting at UTC+13, was one of the first places on earth to ring in the new year, and the kingdom leaned into this distinction. The Pacific clock position that puts Nuku'alofa ahead of almost everywhere else in the world is not an accident of geography but a deliberate choice.

Tonga moved its clocks to UTC+13 in 1995. Before that, the islands sat at UTC-11, on the west side of the International Date Line, meaning Tonga was a day behind its closest neighbors despite being geographically to their east. The change moved Tonga east of the Date Line on the calendar, aligning it with Australia and New Zealand for business and communication purposes, even though the sun tells a different story.

[Tonga](/country/tonga) uses Pacific/Tongatapu in the IANA database, with no daylight saving. At 21 degrees south, the islands have modest seasonal daylight variation. The climate is warm and tropical year-round, with a cyclone season from November to April.

Nuku'alofa is the capital and the main port, on the largest island, Tongatapu. The royal palace, on the waterfront, is occupied by the Tongan monarchy, one of the few remaining Pacific hereditary kingdoms. The church is central to daily life: Sunday is strictly observed, with shops closed and streets quiet in a way that feels genuinely different from other Pacific capitals.
