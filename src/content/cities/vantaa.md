Vantaa is immediately north of Helsinki and contains Helsinki Airport, which means for most travelers it's the first and last piece of [Finland](/country/finland) they touch. The airport runs a global schedule coordinating departures from UTC-12 to UTC+12, all translated into Eastern European Time for local operations.

Finland keeps Eastern European Time, UTC+2 in winter and UTC+3 during summer DST. At 60 degrees north, Vantaa's daylight extremes are significant. Summer solstice brings almost 19 hours of daylight: sunrise before 4am, sunset after 10:30pm. Winter brings less than 6 hours. The DST change is debated intensely in Finland, which has been one of the more vocal EU countries on the question of abolishing the biannual clock change.

Vantaa is a planned postwar city. Helsinki's growth in the 20th century spilled into the surrounding municipalities, and Vantaa grew to house both suburban residential districts and major industrial and logistics facilities. The airport economy gives it a 24-hour character unusual for a Nordic city.

## airport city and 24-hour character

The river Vantaa (Vantaanjoki) that gives the city its name runs into the sea at Vanhankaupunginlahti. The river delta is a migratory bird site, and the spring arrival of birds is one of the informal markers of seasonal transition for local birdwatchers.
