Phnom Penh was built where rivers meet. The Chatomukh, or Four Faces, is the confluence of the Mekong and the Tonle Sap, the point where Cambodia's two great waterways briefly become one. For centuries, this geography made the city a center of trade, and the rivers still set the rhythm of daily life.

[Cambodia](/country/cambodia) uses Indochina Time, UTC+7, with no daylight saving. The same offset runs through [Bangkok](/bangkok) and [Saigon](/ho-chi-minh-city) to the east and west. Sunrise in Phnom Penh comes around 5:45am to 6:15am depending on season, and sunset between 5:45pm and 6:15pm. Close to the equator, the variation is modest.

The morning in Phnom Penh starts at the river. Vendors set up before dawn along Sisowath Quay. The first light comes off the Tonle Sap, which flows north toward the great lake for half the year and reverses direction when the monsoon fills the Mekong in June. That reversal is one of the natural world's stranger clocks.

The Khmer Rouge abolished time in its own way between 1975 and 1979, renaming 1975 "Year Zero" and attempting to restart history. The city is still, in some ways, measuring how far it has come from that particular moment.
