East Honolulu is a residential area on O'ahu's southeastern coast, uphill from Honolulu proper. Like all of [Hawaii](/hawaii), it uses `Pacific/Honolulu`, UTC-10, year-round. Hawaii is one of two US states (the other is Arizona, with exceptions) that do not observe daylight saving time.

## why Hawaii skips daylight saving

Hawaii's case for permanent standard time is geographic. At 21 degrees north, the islands have modest daylight variation: about 13.5 hours in summer and 11 hours in winter. The agricultural and fishing industries that shaped Hawaiian schedules for centuries have always operated by the sun rather than by continental policy. And Hawaii is 2,400 miles from the nearest US mainland state. There is no compelling reason to synchronize with California's clock policy.

UTC-10 creates a distinct timezone identity. When New York is starting its Monday morning, it's still 4am Sunday in East Honolulu. When London is having its afternoon tea, Hawaii is waking up. The islands exist in an earlier time that most of the world never encounters, which contributes to the sense that arrival in Hawaii is genuinely leaving your previous world behind.
