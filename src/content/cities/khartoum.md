Khartoum sits at one of the world's most storied geographic confluences: the junction of the Blue Nile, flowing from Ethiopia's highlands, and the White Nile, arriving from the lakes of equatorial Africa. The city runs on UTC+3, year-round, no daylight saving. The IANA identifier Africa/Khartoum covers the entire country.

Sudan shifted to UTC+3 in 2017, moving from UTC+2, to align more closely with East African partners and its own geographic position. The shift was administratively clean but meant that solar noon in Khartoum now arrives around 12:30pm by the clock rather than 11:30am. The sun moved on paper but not in the sky.

At 16 degrees north, Khartoum is close enough to the equator that seasonal daylight variation is moderate, around 11 to 13 hours. But the city's relationship with time is structured by heat more than light. In summer, temperatures regularly exceed 40C, and the midday hours between 11am and 3pm effectively shut down outdoor activity. Commerce, construction, and street life resume in the late afternoon. The city runs on two activity peaks: morning and evening, with a long forced pause in between.

[Khartoum North](/khartoum-north) across the Nile keeps the same time, as do all Sudanese cities.

[9am in Khartoum is 6am in London](/when/9am-khartoum/london), three hours ahead of GMT.
