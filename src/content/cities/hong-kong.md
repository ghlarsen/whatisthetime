Hong Kong runs on Hong Kong Time, UTC+8, year-round. Daylight saving was last observed in 1979. The fixed clock has held for nearly half a century.

At UTC+8, Hong Kong shares its time with [Beijing](/beijing), [Shanghai](/shanghai), [Singapore](/singapore), [Taipei](/taipei), and [Kuala Lumpur](/kuala-lumpur). This is one of the world's most commercially active timezone clusters.

## The financial bridge

Hong Kong's Stock Exchange opens at 9:30am local time. At that moment, it is 1:30am in [London](/london) and the previous evening in [New York](/new-york). The exchange closes at 4pm, when it is 8am in London and 3am in New York.

This schedule gives Hong Kong a window of overlap with European markets in the afternoon and early evening. Fund managers, traders, and analysts at international banks in Hong Kong work early morning and late evening shifts to cover both Asian and Western trading hours. The city's financial industry runs on a schedule shaped entirely by timezone math.

## The 1979 decision

Hong Kong dropped daylight saving in 1979. The British colony had observed it earlier, but the government decided the energy savings were marginal and the disruption to international business coordination was not worth it. Fixed UTC+8 allowed reliable scheduling with partners across Asia.

When Hong Kong returned to China in 1997, the timezone was unchanged. Both the colonial administration and the post-handover SAR government reached the same conclusion: UTC+8, no adjustments.
