Ségou sits on the Niger River about 240 kilometers northeast of Bamako, the capital of [Mali](/country/mali). It runs on Greenwich Mean Time (UTC+0) all year, the same offset Mali has maintained without daylight saving time throughout its independence.

The city was the capital of the Bambara Empire from the late seventeenth century until the French colonial conquest in 1890. Its history is long and its identity on the Niger River is older than the colonial borders that now define Mali. The river remains central to life here: fishing, transport, trade, and the seasonal flood that once shaped the agricultural calendar.

At 13 degrees north, Ségou sees roughly 13 hours of daylight in June and about 11 in December. The variation is real but the temperatures are extreme enough year-round that the solar geometry matters less than the difference between the dry Harmattan season and the wet season in terms of daily activity patterns.

Ségou is also known for the Festival sur le Niger, an annual arts and culture festival held on the river. The event brings musicians, artists, and visitors from across West Africa and beyond, creating a brief seasonal disruption of the city's usual rhythm.

[Sikasso](/sikasso) to the south and Bamako further west share the same GMT offset.

[9am in Ségou is 9am in London](/when/9am-segou/london) in winter. During British Summer Time, London moves to UTC+1 and the gap opens to one hour.
