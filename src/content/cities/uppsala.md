Uppsala was the religious capital of Sweden before Stockholm grew to dominance, and Uppsala University, founded 1477, is Scandinavia's oldest. The city has never quite stopped being an intellectual center. It sits 70 kilometers north of Stockholm, connected by frequent trains, and maintains its own identity as a university city where the academic calendar still sets the pace.

[Sweden](/country/sweden) keeps Central European Time, UTC+1 in winter and UTC+2 during summer DST. At 60 degrees north, Uppsala's daylight variation is extreme. In June, the sun is up by 3:30am and sets just after 10pm. The sky barely darkens between sunset and sunrise at the solstice. In December, the sun rises after 8:45am and sets before 2:45pm: less than six hours of light.

Uppsala Cathedral, the largest church in Scandinavia, has tolled time for the city since the 15th century. The university's academic terms, Michaelmas and spring, divide the year in the older tradition, and student nations (student associations with their own buildings and schedules) create a micro-temporal layer within the city's rhythm.

Carl Linnaeus, who developed the taxonomic classification system for all living things, was a professor here. His Uppsala garden still grows specimens he catalogued, organized by scientific time rather than seasonal intuition.

[9am in Uppsala is 8am in London](/when/9am-uppsala/london). [Stockholm](/stockholm) and [Gothenburg](/gothenburg) share the same Swedish timezone.
