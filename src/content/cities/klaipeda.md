Klaipėda sits on the Baltic Sea at 55 degrees north latitude, where Lithuania's coast meets a narrow lagoon (the Curonian Lagoon) separated from the open sea by a thin sand spit. The city is Lithuania's only seaport and runs on Eastern European Time, the same clock as [Kaunas](/kaunas) and [Vilnius](/vilnius): UTC+2 in winter, UTC+3 during summer daylight saving.

## Baltic port with a contested past

At this latitude, the light extremes are real. Summer solstice brings nearly 18 hours of daylight, and the sky never goes fully dark. December solstice delivers less than 7 hours. The DST transition in late March is felt sharply here: an hour of morning light exchanged for an hour of evening light. Baltic cultures have long built seasonal rituals around these extremes, from midsummer bonfires (Joninės) to the contemplative pace of dark winter months.

Klaipėda has been German (Memel), Lithuanian, and for a brief period a League of Nations protectorate. Its modern Lithuanian identity was consolidated after 1945. The port handles a significant portion of Lithuanian trade, including energy imports through an offshore LNG terminal, and the city's industrial operations keep unusual hours tied to shipping schedules rather than local solar logic.

The Curonian Spit, across the lagoon from the city, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site where amber washes ashore. Amber dealers in Klaipėda have been trading on their own schedule for millennia.
