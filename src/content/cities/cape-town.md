Cape Town runs on Africa/Johannesburg: UTC+2, South Africa Standard Time, year-round. South Africa last observed daylight saving time in 1944. The fixed UTC+2 offset applies permanently, and there are no signs this will change: DST proposals surface periodically and are consistently rejected.

Cape Town sits at 34 degrees south, giving it significant seasonal daylight variation. December and January (midsummer) see sunset after 8pm, with long golden hours that define the Cape's social culture. June and July (midwinter) bring darkness before 6pm. The wine farms of Stellenbosch and Franschhoek, the coastal walks around the Cape Peninsula, all calibrate their peak activity to summer's extended light.

## At the bottom of Africa

Cape Town is the southwestern anchor of Africa. The Cape of Good Hope is not technically the southernmost point of Africa (Cape Agulhas, 150 kilometers to the southeast, holds that distinction), but it is the symbolic end of the continent. Portuguese navigator Bartolomeu Dias rounded the Cape in 1488. The Dutch East India Company established a refreshment station here in 1652, beginning European settlement.

The Cape's position at the confluence of the Atlantic and Indian Oceans gave it strategic importance for maritime navigation. Ships rounding the Cape needed to synchronize their chronometers after the long South Atlantic crossing. Observatory, one of Cape Town's suburbs, is named after the Royal Observatory established there in 1820 specifically to provide accurate time signals to ships entering Table Bay.

## Summer in winter's months

Cape Town's seasons are inverted from the Northern Hemisphere. December to February is the hot, dry summer. July to September is the cool, wet winter. The long December evenings, with sunset after 8pm, fill the V&A Waterfront, Clifton Beach, and the Atlantic Seaboard promenades. This is the high season for Cape Town tourism, when the city is most alive.

The [time conversion from Cape Town to London](/when/9am-cape-town/london) puts London two hours behind in winter, one in summer when the UK observes BST.
