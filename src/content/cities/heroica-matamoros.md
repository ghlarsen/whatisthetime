Heroica Matamoros sits directly across the Rio Grande from Brownsville, Texas, connected by international bridges. It runs on Central Standard Time, UTC-6 in winter, and Central Daylight Time, UTC-5, in summer. The daylight saving schedule follows US conventions, unlike most of Mexico which uses its own transition dates.

Tamaulipas state, where Matamoros is located, received a special exemption to follow US daylight saving schedules. This keeps Matamoros in sync with Brownsville year-round, which matters enormously for a twin-city economy.

## A twin-city economy

Matamoros and Brownsville are economically inseparable. Maquiladora factories on the Mexican side employ tens of thousands of workers who produce goods for the US market. The international bridges carry commercial trucks, workers, shoppers, and family members continuously through the day.

A timezone mismatch would mean missed shifts, confused schedules, and commercial friction. The synchronized clocks reduce that friction by eliminating one of the most basic coordination problems between the cities.

## The Rio Grande's shifting banks

The Rio Grande that forms the border here has shifted over centuries, creating legal ambiguity about exactly where Mexico ends and the US begins in certain spots. Whatever the surveyors say, the social and economic reality is a single metropolitan area divided by a river and a political line, united by a shared clock.
