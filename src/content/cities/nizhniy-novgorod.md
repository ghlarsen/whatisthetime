Nizhniy Novgorod sits at the confluence of the Volga and Oka rivers, a geographic fact that made it one of Russia's most important trading cities for centuries. The Nizhny Novgorod Fair, held annually from the early 19th century until 1930, was the largest trade fair in the world at its peak, drawing merchants from Europe, Persia, Central Asia, and China. The city's position on the river network made this possible: everything that moved along Russia's interior waterways converged here.

Russia uses Moscow Time, UTC+3, year-round without daylight saving across much of European Russia. The timezone is shared with [Moscow](/moscow) to the west. Nizhniy Novgorod sits about 400 kilometers from Moscow and has no timezone difference from the capital.

At 56 degrees north, the city's seasonal light swing is dramatic. Summer solstice brings nearly 18 hours of daylight and white nights when twilight lingers after midnight. December brings under 7 hours. The Volga freezes in winter, typically in November or December, and the ice break-up in April is a significant seasonal event, marking the end of winter and the beginning of river navigation season.

The city was renamed Gorky during the Soviet period and served as a closed scientific city. It's now open and home to automobile manufacturing (the GAZ plant), one of Russia's largest auto companies, and a growing technology sector.
