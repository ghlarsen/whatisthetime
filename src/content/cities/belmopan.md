Belmopan is one of the world's newer capital cities, built from scratch after Hurricane Hattie destroyed much of [Belize City](/belize-city) in 1961. The government relocated inland, and in 1970 Belmopan became the official capital of what was then British Honduras. It is small, planned, and quiet, and it keeps exactly the same time as the rest of [Belize](/belize): UTC-6, no daylight saving, year-round.

The city sits about 80 kilometers west of the coast, in the Cayo District, at an elevation that keeps it cooler than the humid coastline. The inland location also means slightly more variation in daylight than Belize City, but the difference is modest at this latitude.

## Built for resilience

The decision to move the capital inland was driven by hurricane vulnerability, not administrative preference. Belmopan was designed with government buildings organized around a central square inspired by Maya city planning. That deliberate design makes it the only capital city in the Western Hemisphere explicitly modeled on ancient indigenous architecture.

Time in Belmopan runs on government schedules more than commercial ones. The embassies, ministries, and National Assembly set the rhythm. Civil servants start at 8am and the working day follows a predictable arc. The evening is quiet. Nightlife is sparse. Belmopan sleeps early.

## Timezone context

Belize holds steady at UTC-6 while neighbors observe varying offsets. Guatemala to the west also uses UTC-6 but applied DST intermittently in the past before abandoning it. Mexico's nearby Quintana Roo moved to UTC-5 permanently in 2015. Within Belize, there is no internal timezone variation: [Belize City](/belize-city) and Belmopan are always on the same clock.

The [time difference between Belmopan and London](/when/9am-belmopan/london) is six hours in winter.
