Camagüey is Cuba's third-largest city, sitting in the broad central plains of the island far from either coast. It runs on America/Havana: UTC-5 in winter (Cuba Standard Time), UTC-4 in summer (Cuba Daylight Time). Cuba is one of the few Caribbean nations that observes daylight saving time.

Cuba's DST schedule has sometimes diverged from the North American calendar. The Cuban government has adjusted DST start and end dates for energy conservation reasons, occasionally keeping clocks advanced for extended periods. The inconsistency has been notable enough that the IANA database maintains careful records of Cuba's clock history.

## The maze city

Camagüey is famous for its urban layout, a deliberate maze of narrow, winding streets designed to confuse attacking pirates and colonial-era raiders. Unlike the grid plans of most Spanish colonial cities, Camagüey's streets follow no logical pattern. The city has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2008. Local legend holds that navigation requires either local knowledge or acceptance of getting lost.

The city's tinajones, large ceramic jars used historically for water storage, are scattered throughout the historic center as both functional objects and cultural symbols. Camagüey's 21 historical plazas, linked by these winding streets, give the city an intimate, enclosed urban character.

## Interior Cuba

Away from the Havana coast, Camagüey has a more traditional, slower pace. The cattle economy of Cuba's interior has historically centered on this region. The city's famous ballet company, the Ballet de Camagüey, is one of Cuba's most respected cultural institutions.

The [time conversion from Camagüey to London](/when/9am-camaguey/london) puts London five hours ahead in winter, four in summer. [Havana](/havana) is on the same clock.
