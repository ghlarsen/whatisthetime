Danmarkshavn is not a city in any conventional sense. It is a small Danish meteorological station on Greenland's remote northeastern coast, one of the most isolated permanently staffed outposts on Earth. A handful of Danish meteorologists rotate through, taking weather measurements that feed into Arctic climate models. The IANA database includes it as `America/Danmarkshavn`, UTC+0 year-round, with no daylight saving.

The timezone is geographically unusual. Most of Greenland sits in Western Hemisphere longitudes, where UTC-3 or UTC-4 would be solar-accurate. But Danmarkshavn, by choice or administrative convention, runs on UTC+0, the same as London and [Dakar](/dakar). This is primarily because the station operates in close coordination with Danish institutions, and UTC+0 simplified communication.

At 76.8 degrees north, daylight saving time would be meaningless. The sun stays above the horizon continuously from roughly late April to mid-August. In winter, it doesn't rise at all from late October to mid-February. There is no "saving" of daylight because daylight operates on an entirely different logic at this latitude. Sunrise and sunset are seasonal phenomena, not daily ones.
