Karagandy sits in the dead center of Kazakhstan, on a steppe so vast and flat that the horizon in every direction is an unbroken line. At 50 degrees north latitude, the city sees dramatic seasonal daylight swings: summer evenings stay light past 10pm, while winter afternoons dim before 5. The city runs on Asia/Almaty time, UTC+5, shared with [Almaty](/almaty) to the southeast.

The city grew from coal. Soviet planners built Karagandy in the 1930s around the Karaganda coal basin, one of the largest in the USSR. The mines ran on shifts, not on solar time, and that industrial rhythm shaped the city's relationship with the clock. Shifts began and ended regardless of whether the sun was up or down. The city still carries that round-the-clock working culture.

Kazakhstan abolished daylight saving time in 2005, a decision the government justified as reducing stress on the population and avoiding the productivity dip associated with spring clock changes. Karagandy went from seasonal confusion to permanent UTC+5, and nobody seems to miss the adjustment.

The city is geographically close to the center of Asia, which makes its UTC+5 offset feel approximately right by the sun. Noon arrives fairly close to solar noon, unlike cities pushed far from their natural meridian by political timezone borders.

[9am in Karagandy is 4am in London](/when/9am-karagandy/london), putting it five hours ahead of GMT.
