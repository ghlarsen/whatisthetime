Dublin's relationship with time has a legal peculiarity. The Standard Time Act 1968 defines Irish Standard Time (IST, UTC+1) as the country's standard time, and Greenwich Mean Time (UTC+0) as the winter offset. In effect, Ireland observes "negative daylight saving time": it falls back to a lower offset in winter rather than rising to a higher one. The result is identical to what neighboring countries do, but the legal framing is inverted.

At 53.3 degrees north, Dublin has dramatic daylight variation. Near the summer solstice, sunrise comes before 5am and sunset after 10pm in Dublin. In the winter solstice, sunrise is after 8:30am and sunset before 4:15pm. The extra evening light during IST is genuinely used: outdoor culture, pub gardens, and evening sports all benefit. Ireland's pubs were historically required to close by 11pm; the long summer evenings stretched the last hours of light well past closing time.

[Cork](/cork) to the southwest shares the same clock, though its westerly longitude means the sun sets slightly later there than in Dublin. The whole island runs on a single clock, which is sensible for a country about 480 kilometers from north to south.
