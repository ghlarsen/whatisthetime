Khabarovsk Vtoroy means "Khabarovsk Second," a designation that marks it as the city's secondary railway station district, built to handle Trans-Siberian Railway overflow as the city grew through the Soviet period. It keeps the same time as [Khabarovsk](/khabarovsk): Vladivostok Time, UTC+10, year-round.

## two clocks in your head: local and Moscow time

The Trans-Siberian Railway runs on an interesting temporal split. Timetables and tickets are printed in Moscow Time throughout the entire route, regardless of local time at each station. A train departing Khabarovsk Vtoroy at what locals call 8am is listed on the board as departing at 1am Moscow Time. Travelers have missed trains by confusing the two. Long-distance railway culture here involves maintaining two clocks in your head simultaneously.

The station district itself is more utilitarian than central Khabarovsk, defined by freight yards, maintenance facilities, and the particular dormitory architecture of Soviet rail worker housing. The day begins when the first trains do, well before dawn in winter when UTC+10 means the clock says 6am but the Amur valley is still completely dark.

Russia's permanent shift to UTC+10 (without DST) since 2014 means this rail junction handles scheduling without seasonal adjustment, which simplifies long-route coordination across the country's eleven timezones.

[9am in Khabarovsk Vtoroy is midnight in London](/when/9am-khabarovsk-vtoroy/london), the same offset as its neighbor.
