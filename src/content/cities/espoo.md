Espoo, directly west of Helsinki, is Finland's second-largest city and home to Nokia's headquarters, Aalto University, and a concentration of technology companies that make it one of Scandinavia's foremost innovation centers. It shares `Europe/Helsinki`: EET (UTC+2) in winter and EEST (UTC+3) in summer, following EU DST rules.

At 60.2 degrees north, Espoo shares Helsinki's extreme daylight variation. Near the summer solstice, the sky never fully darkens, with civil twilight persisting through the night. The winter solstice brings fewer than 6 hours of official daylight, and actual useful light even less. The Finnish discussion about eliminating DST is active: the country would likely choose permanent summer time (UTC+3) if EU coordination permitted, preserving the long summer evenings that are central to Finnish cultural life.

Espoo's tech industry runs on global schedules that transcend local daylight patterns. Engineers at Nokia coordinating with teams in San Jose and Singapore experience time as a sequence of calls across time zones rather than as daylight. The UTC+2/+3 offset is just one coordinate in a scheduling grid that spans 20 hours of time zones at a time.
