Ibadan is Nigeria's second-largest city and one of the largest in Africa, with a population that exceeds three million in the metropolitan area. It runs on West Africa Time, UTC+1, year-round, the same as [Lagos](/lagos) and all of Nigeria. No daylight saving.

Ibadan sits about 120 kilometers north of Lagos on a plateau, at a higher elevation than the coastal capital. The two cities share a timezone but have distinct characters: Lagos is commercial and chaotic, Ibadan is the Yoruba cultural heartland, home to the University of Ibadan, Nigeria's oldest university.

## The Yoruba calendar and time

The Yoruba traditionally used a four-day week: Ojó Àìkú, Ojó Ajé, Ojó Ìşégùn, and Ojó Rúbó. This system, tied to market days and religious observances, ran alongside and eventually beneath the colonial seven-day week. Market days in Ibadan's traditional markets still follow a Yoruba calendar logic that exists independently of the civil timezone.

## University city rhythms

The University of Ibadan, established in 1948, shaped the city's social clock. Academic terms, research cycles, and student life impose a structured rhythm on a city that had previously been organized around commerce and the palace of the Oba. The university's presence made Ibadan a city that runs partly on an intellectual schedule: late-night seminars, early-morning lectures, examination periods.
