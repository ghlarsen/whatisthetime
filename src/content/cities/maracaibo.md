Maracaibo sits on the western shore of Lake Maracaibo in northwestern Venezuela, above one of the western hemisphere's largest oil reserves. It runs on Venezuela Standard Time, UTC-4, the fixed offset that [Venezuela](/country/venezuela) has kept year-round since 2007.

Hugo Chávez moved Venezuela's clocks back by 30 minutes in 2007, shifting from UTC-4 to UTC-4:30, citing agricultural rhythms and more efficient use of natural daylight. It was a controversial and internationally unusual move: Venezuela became one of a handful of countries in the world on a half-hour offset.

In 2016, under Nicolás Maduro, Venezuela moved the clocks forward by 30 minutes again, back to UTC-4. The official reason was an electricity crisis: the change was said to save power by aligning with more daylight. The net effect of both changes was a return to the pre-Chávez offset, having spent nine years at UTC-4:30.

Maracaibo's oil infrastructure, the derricks that dot the lake, operate on continuous shifts. The civil clock matters for administration and communication, while the wells themselves run on industrial time.

[9am in Maracaibo is 1pm in London](/when/9am-maracaibo/london) during European winter.
