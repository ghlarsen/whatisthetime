Sarajevo sits in a valley of the Dinaric Alps, surrounded by hills that close in quickly around the city. It runs on Central European Time (UTC+1 in winter, UTC+2 in summer), in line with the rest of [Bosnia and Herzegovina](/country/bosnia-and-herzegovina) and the broader Central European bloc.

But Sarajevo's relationship with time goes deeper than a timezone offset. The city spent centuries as a major center of the Ottoman Empire, then was absorbed by Austro-Hungary in 1878. The Austro-Hungarian administration imposed Western European clock discipline on a city that had organized its day largely around Islamic prayer times. The old and new systems overlapped for years. The Gazi Husrev-beg Mosque's clock tower still stands in Baščaršija, the old bazaar district, once set to Ottoman time measured from sunset rather than midnight.

The 1914 assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand in Sarajevo is the moment that crashed European political time into a new era. The precise hour, 10:45am on June 28, is fixed in histories of the twentieth century's first catastrophe.

The siege of Sarajevo from 1992 to 1996 imposed its own temporal logic: the clock kept running, but the shelling schedule, the sniper fire patterns, and the rotation of UN convoys created a parallel time system that residents navigated alongside official hours.

[9am in Sarajevo is 8am in London](/when/9am-sarajevo/london) in winter, one hour behind Central European Time.
