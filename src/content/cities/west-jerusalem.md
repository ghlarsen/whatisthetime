West Jerusalem is the Jewish-majority part of the city that [Israel](/country/israel) designates as its capital, where the Knesset, Supreme Court, and government ministries are located. The city's eastern part, including the Old City, is disputed territory. The distinction between West and East Jerusalem is political and legal, but the clocks are the same on both sides.

Israel Standard Time, UTC+2 in winter and UTC+3 in summer, governs the civil calendar. The DST transitions don't follow European schedules exactly: the start and end dates shift slightly based on the Jewish calendar, ensuring that the clock change does not interfere with major religious observances. The specific dates vary year to year.

## three calendars, overlapping rest days

Three religious calendars layer over the civil one in Jerusalem. The Jewish Shabbat runs from Friday sunset to Saturday nightfall, the Islamic Jumu'ah prayer is on Friday, and the Christian Sabbath is Sunday. For municipal services, these overlapping rest days create a genuinely complex schedule where different parts of the city operate on different weekly rhythms.

The Western Wall plaza falls silent on Shabbat and holidays in a way it does not at other times. The call to prayer from the Dome of the Rock crosses the entire Old City five times daily. The church bells of the Christian Quarter ring their own hours. The city is, among other things, a museum of how different traditions mark time.

[9am in West Jerusalem is 7am in London](/when/9am-west-jerusalem/london). [Tel Aviv](/tel-aviv) on the coast shares the same timezone.
