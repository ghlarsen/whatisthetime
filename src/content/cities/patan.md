Pātan, also known as Lalitpur, shares the Kathmandu Valley with Kathmandu and Bhaktapur, three cities whose medieval cores are packed with UNESCO-listed temples, courtyards, and Newari architecture that survived relatively intact until the 2015 earthquake and has been partially rebuilt since. Pātan's Durbar Square is considered by many the finest of the three.

Nepal uses Nepal Time, UTC+5:45, the only UTC+5:45 offset in the world. The 45-minute increment was chosen in 1956 to split the difference between Indian Standard Time (UTC+5:30) and time zones further east. It places Nepal 15 minutes ahead of India, a deliberate assertion of national distinctiveness from a country that surrounds it on three sides.

At 27.7 degrees north in the Himalayan foothills, Pātan's daylight ranges from about 10.5 hours in December to 13.5 hours in June. The valley's 1,400-meter elevation moderates the temperature relative to the lowland Terai, and the seasons are distinct: monsoon from June through September brings heavy rain and humidity, winter brings cold fog and frost, spring and autumn are the clearest and most pleasant seasons.

Pātan's Newari community, the indigenous people of the Kathmandu Valley, maintains a calendar of festivals that is among the most dense in the world. Major festivals occur nearly monthly, with elaborate preparations that involve the entire community.
