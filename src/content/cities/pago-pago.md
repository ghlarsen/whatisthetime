Pago Pago sits in one of the South Pacific's most dramatic natural harbors, a deep volcanic bay surrounded by steep green mountains. It is also one of the most remote populated territories in the United States, and one of the last places on earth to mark the close of each day.

American Samoa uses Samoa Standard Time, UTC-11, year-round with no daylight saving. The IANA identifier is Pacific/Pago_Pago. While independent Samoa (formerly Western Samoa) sits just 70 kilometers to the northwest, the two are on opposite sides of the International Date Line, with Western Samoa at UTC+13 and American Samoa at UTC-11. The same Pacific Ocean separates places that are, on the calendar, a full day apart.

The history is political: Western Samoa crossed the Date Line in 2011 to align itself commercially with Australia and New Zealand. American Samoa remained at UTC-11, maintaining alignment with the US West Coast 2,600 kilometers away. The result is a geographic absurdity where near neighbors operate on dates 24 hours apart.

## the harbor, the canneries, and the date line quirk

At 14 degrees south, Pago Pago's daylight varies modestly: roughly 11 to 13 hours across the year. The harbor is one of the world's deepest natural ports, and the tuna canneries that once dominated the economy ran on processing schedules tied to the fishing fleet's arrivals.
