Blagoveshchensk is the capital of Amur Oblast in Russia's Far East. It runs on Asia/Chita: UTC+9, year-round. Russia has observed no daylight saving since 2014.

The city's most remarkable time feature isn't its own clock. It's what's directly across the Amur River. Heihe, a city in China's Heilongjiang Province, sits on the opposite bank, sometimes only a few hundred meters away. China uses a single national timezone: UTC+8, Beijing Time. Russia uses UTC+9 in this region. The result is that two cities facing each other across a river, close enough to see the apartment buildings and traffic, operate on clocks one hour apart.

## The river border

The Amur River has served as the Russian-Chinese border here since the Treaty of Aigun in 1858. The two cities developed separately under very different political and economic systems. Soviet-era Blagoveshchensk was closed to foreign visitors. After the Cold War, cross-river trade opened. There are ferry services and a bridge connecting the two cities, completed in 2022 after decades of planning.

Crossing from Blagoveshchensk to Heihe means setting your clock back one hour. Crossing back means setting it forward. Day traders, tourists, and business visitors navigate this time jump as part of daily life near the border.

## Agriculture and the Trans-Siberian

Amur Oblast is one of Russia's agricultural regions, known for soy cultivation, an unlikely crop this far north. The region's farming calendar aligns with the UTC+9 offset. [Belogorsk](/belogorsk) to the north uses the same clock, as does [Chita](/chita) further west.

The [time difference between Blagoveshchensk and London](/when/9am-blagoveshchensk/london) is nine hours in winter, eight in summer when the UK observes BST.
