George Town is the capital of the Cayman Islands and one of the world's major offshore financial centers. It runs on UTC-5 all year, the same as US Eastern Standard Time in winter. When the United States shifts to daylight saving in spring, New York moves to UTC-4 while George Town stays at UTC-5. From March to November, the Caymans run an hour behind New York.

This matters for finance. The Cayman Islands host more registered companies than residents. The financial industry operates across multiple timezones, and the fixed UTC-5 offset means traders and lawyers in George Town are always calculating the delta to [New York](/new-york) during business hours.

## Caribbean latitude and consistent daylight

George Town sits at 19 degrees North, deep in the tropics. Day length varies minimally across the year: about an hour difference between the longest and shortest days. With such consistency, there is no compelling solar argument for daylight saving. The fixed clock makes practical sense.

The Caribbean sun is strong year-round. Residents and the diving tourism industry both rely on predictable afternoon conditions. Scuba schedules, boat tours, and beach operations all follow the sun more than the clock.

## A town built on financial time

The offshore finance industry operates in precise time windows. Documents must be executed, wire transfers confirmed, and registrations filed within specific hours. The registrar, the banks, and the law firms all run on a schedule synchronized to the UTC-5 offset. This financial precision coexists with the island's relaxed tourism culture in a city of fewer than 35,000 people.
