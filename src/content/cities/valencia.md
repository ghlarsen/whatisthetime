Valencia, [Venezuela](/country/venezuela) (distinct from Valencia, Spain), is the country's second-largest city and the center of its automobile and industrial manufacturing sector. It sits in the fertile Carabobo valley, and the Battle of Carabobo fought nearby in 1821 was the decisive engagement that secured Venezuelan independence from Spain.

Venezuela keeps Venezuela Standard Time, UTC-4. The country's timezone history is unusual. In 2007, President Hugo Chávez moved Venezuela's clocks back by 30 minutes to UTC-4:30, which he said would give more daylight to schoolchildren in the morning and align the country's solar noon more accurately with Venezuelan geography. In 2016, after Chávez's death, the Maduro government moved the clocks forward 30 minutes back to UTC-4, citing energy conservation.

At 10 degrees north, Valencia has minimal daylight variation: sunrise and sunset move by only about 45 minutes across the year. The clock adjustments of 2007 and 2016 had more political and practical significance than any seasonal logic could supply.

Valencia's industrial rhythm follows factory schedules, and the auto assembly plants that anchor the city's economy run on coordinated shift systems aligned with Caracas business hours.

[9am in Valencia is 1pm in London](/when/9am-valencia/london). [Caracas](/caracas) keeps the same Venezuela Standard Time.
