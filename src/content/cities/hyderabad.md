Hyderabad runs on India Standard Time, UTC+5:30, the single timezone that covers all of India. No daylight saving. India has used UTC+5:30 since 1947, when the offset was set at independence to resolve the historical patchwork of colonial timekeeping.

The half-hour offset is deliberate: India's vast east-west extent spans nearly 30 degrees of longitude, meaning the solar time difference between the eastern and western edges exceeds two hours. UTC+5:30 splits the difference, fitting no one perfectly but working reasonably for the country as a whole.

## HITEC City and the overnight economy

Hyderabad's HITEC City district is one of India's major technology and business process outsourcing hubs. Companies here provide software development, customer support, and data services to clients in the United States and Europe.

At UTC+5:30, Hyderabad's 9am is 3:30am in London and 10:30pm the previous evening in New York. This creates a workforce that often works evening or night shifts to overlap with client business hours. The tech industry's overnight economy runs on [London](/london)'s and [New York](/new-york)'s clocks from a building in Hyderabad.

## The Nizam's time and the biryani clock

Hyderabad was the seat of the Nizam, one of the wealthiest rulers in the world during the colonial era. The Nizam's court ran on its own schedule: elaborate meals at specific hours, prayer times aligned with the sun, court ceremonies timed by tradition. Hyderabadi biryani, the slow-cooked rice dish, has its own logic of time: the dum cooking process requires hours of low heat, and no clock can rush it.
