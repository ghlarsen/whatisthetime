Brandon is Manitoba's second-largest city, sitting on the Assiniboine River in the heart of the Canadian prairie. It runs on America/Winnipeg: UTC-6 in winter (Central Standard Time), UTC-5 in summer (Central Daylight Time), following Canada's standard DST schedule.

At 50 degrees north latitude, Brandon experiences dramatic seasonal daylight variation. In June, the sun sets past 9:30pm local time. In December, it's dark before 4:30pm. The prairie horizon amplifies these transitions: there are no mountains or forests to soften the sunrise or hold back the sunset. The sky is enormous and the light changes are stark.

## Prairie grain and railway time

Brandon grew because of wheat. The Canadian Pacific Railway reached here in 1881, and the city became a major grain handling and processing center for Manitoba's agricultural hinterland. The CPR set local time in prairie Canada before there were formal provincial or federal standards: railway time was simply the time that mattered for commerce and travel.

The city's agricultural economy still shapes its temporal rhythms. Harvest season in August and September overrides normal schedules. Extended working hours during the short harvest window, before the first frost ends the growing season, are a prairie tradition that persists even in an era of mechanized farming.

## Manitoba and the Saskatchewan comparison

Saskatchewan, directly to Brandon's west, uses Central Standard Time year-round and does not observe DST. This means that for about six months of the year, Brandon is one hour ahead of its western neighbor. The Saskatchewan-Manitoba border is one of Canada's internal timezone transitions: crossing it in summer means changing your clock.

The [time conversion from Brandon to London](/when/9am-brandon/london) is six hours behind in winter, five in summer. [Winnipeg](/winnipeg) is on the same clock.
