Most of Mexico observes daylight saving time on its own schedule. Ciudad Juárez does not. Sitting directly across the Rio Grande from El Paso, Texas, the city adopted US-style DST transitions in 2022, aligning its clocks with its American neighbor rather than the rest of [Mexico](/mexico). This is why the IANA database has a dedicated entry, `America/Ciudad_Juarez`, separate from `America/Chihuahua`.

The practical reason is economic integration. The maquiladora factories on the Mexican side supply American companies across the river. Workers cross the border daily. Managers schedule calls between offices. When El Paso's clocks shifted and Juárez stayed put, the one-hour gap cost real money in missed handoffs and scheduling confusion. Aligning the clocks removed the friction.

The city's border location means its working day stretches across two legal time systems: at base, UTC-7 in winter and UTC-6 in summer, matching [El Paso](/el-paso) on the other side of the fence. At the same time, [Mexico City](/mexico-city) and most of the country sit in a different offset and shift on a different date. Ciudad Juárez chose proximity over national consistency.
