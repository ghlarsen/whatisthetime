Boujdour is a small coastal city in Western Sahara, a territory on Africa's Atlantic coast that Morocco administers but whose status is disputed under international law. The Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic (SADR) claims the territory, and the UN regards it as a non-self-governing territory. The IANA timezone for the region is Africa/El_Aaiun, named after the territory's largest city.

The timezone situation is complicated by Morocco's unusual approach to DST. Morocco observes Western European Time (UTC+0) during parts of the year and Central European Time (UTC+1) during others. The adjustments are irregular and tied to Ramadan: Morocco reverts to UTC+0 during the Islamic holy month, then returns to UTC+1 afterward. The exact schedule changes year to year depending on when Ramadan falls. Western Sahara follows Morocco's lead.

## A disputed desert coast

Boujdour sits on one of the most inhospitable stretches of the Atlantic coast, the edge of the Sahara Desert meeting the ocean. The Canary Current running offshore keeps the waters cool and the coast foggy. For centuries, this stretch was called the "Cape of Fear" by European navigators because of its strong currents and shallow waters.

The phosphate deposits of Western Sahara and the rich offshore fisheries are the primary economic drivers that make Morocco's claim to the territory worth defending. Boujdour's small fishing harbor participates in this economy.

## Living under two claims

Residents of Boujdour navigate daily life under Moroccan administrative structures while the political status of the territory remains unresolved. They use Moroccan currency, Moroccan phone networks, and Moroccan clock time.
