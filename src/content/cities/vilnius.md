Vilnius has one of the largest surviving baroque Old Towns in Europe, a UNESCO World Heritage site, built across several hills and river bends. The city was the capital of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania, one of medieval Europe's largest states, and its architectural layers reflect centuries of Polish, Lithuanian, Russian, Jewish, and German influence.

[Lithuania](/country/lithuania) keeps Eastern European Time, UTC+2 in winter and UTC+3 during summer DST. Lithuania's timezone history includes the Soviet period, when all Baltic states were placed on Moscow Time (UTC+3, or UTC+4 with Soviet summer time), regardless of geographic position. After independence in 1990, Lithuania shifted to Central European Time briefly before settling on Eastern European Time, a more geographically appropriate choice.

At 54 degrees north, Vilnius has strong seasonal daylight variation. June brings nearly 17 hours of daylight. December gives the city about 7 hours. The long summer evenings feed the city's outdoor café culture and the courtyard concerts that are a feature of the Old Town in summer.

## layered calendars and Baltic identity

Vilnius has a particularly significant Jewish history: it was called "Jerusalem of Lithuania" before World War II. The Jewish community's calendar and the Lithuanian Catholic calendar layered complex time references over the civil one for centuries.

[9am in Vilnius is 7am in London](/when/9am-vilnius/london). [Riga](/riga) and [Tallinn](/tallinn) to the north share the same Baltic timezone.
