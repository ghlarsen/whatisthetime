Baltiysk holds a distinction that would be geographically impossible anywhere else: it is Russia's westernmost city, and it is entirely surrounded by NATO members. The Kaliningrad exclave, a piece of Russian territory on the Baltic Sea between Poland and Lithuania, was captured from Germany in 1945. Baltiysk, formerly Pillau, is its main port and the home base of Russia's Baltic Fleet.

The timezone is Kaliningrad Time, UTC+2 year-round. The IANA identifier is Europe/Kaliningrad. Russia abolished daylight saving in 2014, so UTC+2 is the permanent clock here.

Kaliningrad Time is unique in Russia's timezone system: it is the only Russian timezone that is behind Moscow. Moscow is UTC+3. Kaliningrad is UTC+2. Everywhere else in Russia's vast eastern extension is ahead of Moscow. Kaliningrad is behind it.

The exclave's geographic position means that local time aligns with neighboring Poland and Lithuania (both UTC+1/UTC+2 with DST) for part of the year and diverges when those countries' clocks change. In summer, Poland and Lithuania move to UTC+2, matching Kaliningrad. In winter, they drop back to UTC+1, moving one hour behind Kaliningrad.

Baltiysk is a closed military city, access-restricted. The Baltic Fleet's presence shapes the city's character entirely.
