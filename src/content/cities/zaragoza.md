Zaragoza sits at the intersection of the Ebro River and the Camino de Santiago pilgrimage route in the heart of Aragon. The Basílica del Pilar, standing on the Ebro bank, is one of Spain's most visited religious sites, drawing millions of pilgrims and tourists annually. The Pilar Fiesta in October is [Spain](/country/spain)'s most observed national religious celebration outside Holy Week.

Spain keeps Central European Time, UTC+1 in winter and UTC+2 during summer DST. Like all of Spain, Zaragoza sits at a longitude closer to the Greenwich meridian than to Central Europe, but has run on CET since the Franco government aligned Spain with Nazi Germany and fascist Italy in 1940. The solar clock and the civil clock are permanently out of sync: solar noon in Zaragoza falls around 1:30-2pm in winter.

The result is Spain's famously late daily schedule. Lunch at 2-3pm is standard. Dinner at 9-10pm. The Pilar Fiesta evening processions can run past midnight to streets still full of families with children.

## late lunches and the Franco-era clock

Zaragoza was a Roman city (Caesar Augusta), then a Moorish capital (Saraqusta), then the seat of the Crown of Aragon. Each civilization left a temporal layer in its architecture and its saints' days.

[9am in Zaragoza is 8am in London](/when/9am-zaragoza/london). [Madrid](/madrid) and [Barcelona](/barcelona) share the same Spanish timezone.
