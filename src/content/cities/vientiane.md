Vientiane is consistently described as one of Southeast Asia's most relaxed capitals, and the observation is accurate. The city sits on a bend of the Mekong River across from Thailand, and its pace is noticeably slower than Bangkok, Hanoi, or Phnom Penh. The Communist government, which has run [Laos](/country/laos) since 1975, has maintained a cautious, gradual approach to development that has kept the city's colonial-era scale intact.

Indochina Time, UTC+7, is the clock, with no DST. Thailand directly across the river keeps the same time. At 18 degrees north, Vientiane's daylight variation is moderate, and the seasonal distinction here is defined by the monsoon (May to October) and dry season (November to April) rather than by light cycles.

The Pha That Luang stupa, the national symbol of Laos, was built in 1566 and rebuilt multiple times. The Buddhist calendar and the Buddhist festivals mark the year's key dates: Boun Pi Mai Lao (Lao New Year) in April, Boun Ok Phansa at the end of Buddhist Lent, and the That Luang Festival in November. These religious calendars layer over the civil one.

The Mekong itself functions as a clock of sorts: high season from November to February when the river is low and the sandbars appear, wet season when the river rises 10 meters or more.

[9am in Vientiane is 2am in London](/when/9am-vientiane/london). [Thakhèk](/thakhek) to the south shares the same Indochina Time.
