Ouagadougou, known locally as Ouaga, is the capital of Burkina Faso and one of West Africa's more distinct cities, less shaped by French colonial grandeur than Dakar or Abidjan and more defined by its Mossi kingdom history and its position at the center of the Sahelian trade networks. The Grand Marché and the surrounding areas move with a density and energy that the wide colonial avenues elsewhere in the city don't predict.

[Burkina Faso](/country/burkina-faso) runs on Greenwich Mean Time, UTC+0, year-round. The IANA identifier Africa/Ouagadougou designates the capital. No daylight saving applies. At 12.4 degrees north, the city's daylight ranges from about 11 to 13 hours across the year.

The Sahel has two seasons that matter more than the clock: the dry season (October through May) when the harmattan brings dusty air from the north and temperatures can exceed 40 degrees, and the wet season (June through September) when the rains arrive and the landscape briefly turns green. The mango harvest in April and May is one of the year's most anticipated moments: the mangoes come before the rains, during the hottest part of the year, and they are extraordinary.

The FESPACO Pan-African Film and Television Festival, held in Ouagadougou every two years in February, is the largest film festival on the African continent, running its own cinematic clock for two weeks.
