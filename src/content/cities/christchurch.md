Christchurch is New Zealand's second-largest city and the main center of the South Island. It runs on Pacific/Auckland: UTC+12 in winter (April to September), UTC+13 in summer (September to April), following New Zealand's DST schedule. The southern summer brings extraordinarily long days.

At 43 degrees south, Christchurch has significant seasonal daylight variation: December sunsets arrive after 9pm. June sunsets fall before 5:15pm. The summer clock at UTC+13 pushes sunset even later than the natural solar time would suggest, giving extremely long summer evenings.

## Among the first to see the new year

New Zealand is one of the first countries to enter each new day. When it's midnight New Zealand Summer Time (UTC+13), it's 11am in London and 6am in New York on the same day. When Christchurch is celebrating the new year, most of the world is still in the previous year. The city's New Year's celebrations are broadcast globally as among the first.

This temporal position is an unusual cultural feature. Christchurch, like [Auckland](/auckland), exists in a kind of temporal frontier: it experiences dates before most of the world's population.

## The earthquake city

The 2011 Christchurch earthquake, at 12:51pm on February 22, struck at the worst possible time: lunch hour, when the central city was full of people. The timing, midday on a weekday, meant the streets and restaurants were occupied. The earthquake killed 185 people, concentrated in specific buildings that failed catastrophically.

The rebuilding of central Christchurch has been a multi-decade project. The Transitional Cathedral, made of cardboard tubes, and the rebuilt Cathedral Square are part of a reimagined city center that is still taking shape.

The [time conversion from Christchurch to London](/when/9am-christchurch/london) puts London twelve hours behind in winter. [Chatham](/chatham) Islands use UTC+12:45 in winter.
