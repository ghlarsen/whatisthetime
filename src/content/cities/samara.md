Samara has its own timezone: `Europe/Samara`, UTC+4, year-round. This sets it apart from Moscow (UTC+3) to the west. Russia's 2014 decision to abolish daylight saving created a permanent UTC+4 for the Samara region, which had been UTC+4 in summer before that. The result is that Samara is permanently on summer time relative to the old calendar, which means long summer evenings and late winter sunrises.

At 53 degrees north on the Volga, Samara has extreme Russian seasons. Summer is warm and long-eveninged. January is cold and dark. The Volga's embankment is one of Russia's longest riverfront promenades: in summer it fills with people walking, cycling, and watching the river traffic; in winter it is snowbound and mostly empty.

[Russia](/country/russia) kept this UTC+4 zone for the Samara region after the 2014 reform. The city of Samara had been on Samara Time (UTC+4) in summer and UTC+3 in winter. Keeping UTC+4 year-round gave them permanent late sunsets in summer (after 9pm in June) at the cost of very late winter sunrises (after 9:30am in December).

## Stalin's bunker and wartime capital

During World War II, Samara (then called Kuibyshev) served as the alternative capital of the Soviet Union, with government ministries relocated there after the German advance threatened Moscow. Stalin's bunker, built beneath the city, is preserved as a museum.
