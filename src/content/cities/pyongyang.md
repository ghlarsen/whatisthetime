In August 2015, North Korea moved its clocks back by 30 minutes, creating Pyongyang Time at UTC+8:30. The government called it a rejection of "wicked Japanese imperialists" who had imposed Japan Standard Time on the Korean peninsula in 1912. The move was ideologically charged and practically inconvenient: the two Koreas, already separated by politics, now differed by 30 minutes in addition to everything else.

Then, in May 2018, three days before a historic inter-Korean summit, North Korea moved its clocks forward again, returning to UTC+9, the same as [Seoul](/seoul) and the rest of Korea. It was a gesture toward possible reconciliation. The clocks were synchronized. The summit happened. The deeper divisions remained.

[North Korea](/country/north-korea) now uses the `Asia/Pyongyang` IANA identifier, currently set to UTC+9. The IANA database carries the full history of the Pyongyang Time experiment between the two changes.

Pyongyang sits at 39 degrees north, with real seasons. Summer is warm and humid. Winter is cold and dry. The city is built for display: wide boulevards, grand monuments, the Juche Tower on the Taedong River bank. The pace of daily life in Pyongyang is visible to very few outsiders.
