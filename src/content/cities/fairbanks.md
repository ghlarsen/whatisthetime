Fairbanks, Alaska's second-largest city, sits at 64.8 degrees north in the state's interior. It runs on `America/Anchorage`, UTC-9 in winter (AKST) and UTC-8 in summer (AKDT), with standard US DST dates. But the clock is almost irrelevant to what the sky is doing.

Near the summer solstice, the sun rises at 2:58am AKDT and sets at 12:47am the next morning: nearly 22 hours of above-horizon sun. The weeks around the solstice are the Midnight Sun era, when it never fully gets dark. Fairbanks hosts the Midnight Sun Baseball Game each June 21st, an annual event where teams play an outdoor game at midnight with no artificial lights, a tradition dating to 1906.

By contrast, at the winter solstice, daylight in Fairbanks lasts just 3 hours and 42 minutes. December is dark from mid-afternoon. The University of Alaska Fairbanks, the city's largest employer, runs full schedules through this darkness. Residents use full-spectrum lights and learn to detach sleep from the sky's state. The clock at UTC-9 (-8 in summer) is the one navigable fixed point when the sun refuses to cooperate.
