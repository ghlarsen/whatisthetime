Havana runs on Cuba Standard Time, UTC-5, in winter, and Cuba Daylight Time, UTC-4, in summer. Cuba observes daylight saving, but the transition dates are announced by the Cuban government each year rather than following a fixed rule. This means the exact change dates must be checked annually.

Cuba's timezone practices have been variable. The country has occasionally extended or shortened daylight saving periods based on energy conservation needs. During periods of electricity shortage, the government has adjusted clock time to shift demand patterns.

## Havana and the American timezone gap

Before 1959, Cuba's economic and social ties to the United States made timezone alignment straightforward. American businesses, hotels, and tourists set the commercial clock. After the revolution, Cuba maintained UTC-5/-4 but developed independent scheduling culture.

Today, Havana's timezone creates an interesting situation: [Miami](/miami) and Havana are geographically close, historically intertwined, and share the UTC-5/-4 offset, yet they operate in very different economic and political contexts on the same clock.

## Solar time and the revolutionary schedule

At 23 degrees North, Havana has moderate seasonal variation: about 3 hours of difference in day length between solstices. UTC-5 puts solar noon at around 12:10pm, a natural fit. Havana's daily rhythm traditionally includes a long midday break, the siesta pattern common across Spanish-heritage cities, with late lunches and late evenings.
