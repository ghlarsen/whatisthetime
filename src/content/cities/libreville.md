Libreville sits almost exactly on the equator, at 0.4 degrees north, on the estuary of the Gabon River where it meets the Atlantic. The city runs on Africa/Libreville time: UTC+1, year-round. No daylight saving.

At essentially the equator, Libreville has the world's most stable daylength: sunrise around 6am and sunset around 6pm, every single day of the year, with perhaps a 5-minute variation. No season changes the available light. The equatorial position is geographically striking in its temporal consistency.

## oil wealth, freed slaves, and dual schedules

The city's name means "free town" in French, given when it was established in 1849 as a settlement for freed slaves returned to Africa by French naval ships. That original identity as a place of freed people has given way to a modern oil-economy capital where the extreme wealth of the oil sector and the poverty of the broader population coexist uneasily.

Gabon is one of the wealthier countries in sub-Saharan Africa per capita, sustained by oil and manganese exports. The expatriate oil industry workforce creates a dual-schedule city: international companies run on hours tied to European headquarters, while the Gabonese majority operates on a different clock.
