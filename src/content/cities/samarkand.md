Samarkand is among the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world. Greek geographers knew it as Maracanda. Alexander the Great captured it in 329 BC. Genghis Khan destroyed it in 1220. Timur (Tamerlane) rebuilt it as the capital of the Timurid Empire in the 14th century and filled it with the most extraordinary Islamic architecture of the age: the Registan, the Shah-i-Zinda necropolis, the Bibi-Khanym Mosque. The Registan's three madrasas still face each other across their plaza.

[Uzbekistan](/country/uzbekistan) uses Uzbekistan Time, UTC+5, year-round without daylight saving. Samarkand shares this fixed clock with [Tashkent](/tashkent) to the northeast. The country runs on a single timezone.

Samarkand was the great node of the Silk Road, the overland trade routes connecting China to the Mediterranean. Camel caravans arrived and departed on schedules set by the stars, the seasons, and the availability of water. The city's fortune was built on being the place where East met West. Time here was measured in caravan days and the lunar Islamic calendar.

The Afrosiab archaeological site north of the old city contains the ruins of pre-Islamic Samarkand, buried under centuries of deposit. Excavation continues. The city's past keeps being uncovered.
