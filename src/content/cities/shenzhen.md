Shenzhen sits on [China](/country/china)'s southern coast, directly across the border from Hong Kong. It runs on China Standard Time (UTC+8) year-round. In 1980, when Shenzhen was designated China's first Special Economic Zone, it had a population of roughly 30,000. Today the metropolitan area exceeds 17 million. The clock was the same through all of it.

The speed of Shenzhen's growth is a temporal story as much as an economic one. Buildings that would take years to permit and construct in European cities have gone up here in months. Tech product cycles that take eighteen months elsewhere compress to six in Shenzhen's hardware ecosystem. The city's relationship with time is one of compression and acceleration.

Shenzhen is where most of the world's consumer electronics are manufactured, designed, and refined. Huawei, DJI, Tencent, and hundreds of hardware startups cluster here. The Huaqiangbei electronics market, the world's largest component market, operates on schedules tied to factory shifts and delivery windows more than any daily rhythm.

Hong Kong, just across the border, also runs on UTC+8, which keeps cross-border business scheduling simple. The political boundary between the two systems exists, but the clocks agree.

[Shanghai](/shanghai) to the north shares the same China Standard Time.

[9am in Shenzhen is 1am in London](/when/9am-shenzhen/london) in winter, 2am during British Summer Time.
