Dubai runs on UTC+4, year-round, no adjustment. The IANA identifier `Asia/Dubai` covers the UAE. Gulf Standard Time has never included daylight saving transitions, and the stable offset is one of the elements that makes Dubai function as a global transit and business hub.

UTC+4 sits precisely between European and Asian business hours in a way that is commercially useful. Dubai's banks and trading floors open while London is still asleep (4am London time) and remain open into Asian afternoon hours. The three-way overlap between Gulf, European, and Asian working hours is compressed into Dubai's single clock. Emirates airline uses this as a scheduling advantage: Dubai time lets the airline bridge East-West travel with layover times that suit both directions.

## Sunday mornings and European rest days

The work week in Dubai runs Sunday through Thursday, with Friday and Saturday as the weekend, aligning with Islamic tradition. This means Dubai's "Monday morning" is European Sunday evening. For international business partners, this creates persistent scheduling puzzles. A Dubai trader's first working day of the week is their European counterpart's day of rest. UTC+4's stability at least means this asymmetry is constant and predictable.
