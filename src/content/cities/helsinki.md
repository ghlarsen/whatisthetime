Helsinki runs on Eastern European Time, UTC+2 in winter and Eastern European Summer Time, UTC+3, from late March to late October. Finland has used this offset since the Soviet era established regional timezone alignment.

At 60 degrees North latitude, Helsinki has some of the most extreme seasonal daylight variation of any European capital. Midsummer days are almost 19 hours long. Midwinter days last barely 6 hours. The dramatic swing makes daylight saving both more compelling and more disorienting than further south.

## Finland and the EU clock change debate

In 2018, the European Commission proposed eliminating mandatory daylight saving time across EU member states, prompted partly by a petition signed by 4.6 million Europeans. Finland was among the most vocal advocates for ending the practice: Finnish public opinion surveys consistently showed strong support for stopping the clock changes.

The debate stalled over which permanent time to adopt. Should Europe stay on summer time (UTC+3 for Helsinki) or winter time (UTC+2)? Countries disagreed. The EU has not resolved the question. Finland continues changing its clocks twice a year.

## Midsummer and the solar calendar

Midsummer, Juhannus, is Finland's most important cultural festival, tied entirely to the summer solstice and celebrated with bonfires by lakeshores. This is solar time as cultural fact: regardless of what UTC+3 says, the celebration marks the astronomical event. Thousands of Helsinkians leave the city for lakeside cottages.
