Kaunas was Lithuania's provisional capital from 1920 to 1939, while Vilnius remained under Polish administration. The city developed a dense interwar modernism during those years, a distinct architectural identity built quickly and confidently on the assumption that Kaunas would matter. It still does. Today it's Lithuania's second city, running on the same clock as [Vilnius](/vilnius): Eastern European Time, UTC+2 in winter, UTC+3 during daylight saving.

## rivers, trade routes, and seasonal extremes

Lithuania sits at about 55 degrees north, where the seasonal daylight difference is significant. Kaunas summers see sunrise around 5am and sunset past 10pm. December delivers less than 8 hours of light. The DST switch in late March and late October is felt here more sharply than in southern Europe.

The Nemunas and Neris rivers meet at Kaunas, and the Old Town at their confluence has been a trading point for centuries. Medieval merchants here had to reconcile local solar time with the clocks of Königsberg, Riga, and Warsaw. Modern Kaunas simply uses EU standard time like its neighbors.

Lithuania joined the European Union in 2004, which synchronized its DST transitions with the EU-wide schedule. There has been EU-level discussion about abolishing daylight saving entirely, but as of 2026, the clocks still move twice a year.

[9am in Kaunas is 7am in London](/when/9am-kaunas/london) in winter, and 8am during EU summer time.
